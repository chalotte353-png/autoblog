'The Rocky Horror Show' Broadway Review: A Time Warp

Broadway's long-awaited revival of 'The Rocky Horror Show' has arrived with a fiendishly talented cast led by Luke Evans, Rachel Dratch, and Juliette Lewis, and the result is nothing short of a theatrical revelation. Richard O'Brien's beloved cult classic finds new energy and irreverent joy in this bold new production that honors the source material while injecting fresh electricity into every sequined moment. This is the kind of show that reminds audiences why live theater remains an irreplaceable experience.
Olivia Brown
Film Critic

Broadway's Most Anticipated Revival Does Not Disappoint

There is something deeply satisfying about watching a production know exactly what it is and commit to that vision with every fiber of its fishnet-clad being. The new Broadway revival of The Rocky Horror Show, now playing at a Midtown Manhattan theater, is precisely that kind of show. From the opening bars of "Science Fiction Double Feature" to the final shimmying strains of "Super Heroes," this production declares its intentions boldly and then exceeds every expectation with gleeful, glittering abandon.

Richard O'Brien's 1973 rock musical has, of course, been a cultural touchstone for generations of devoted fans. The story of naive couple Brad and Janet stumbling into the eccentric castle of the alien transvestite scientist Dr. Frank-N-Furter has spawned one of cinema's most enduring cult films and countless stage revivals worldwide. Yet there was every reason to approach this new Broadway incarnation with cautious optimism. The question was never whether the material could still entertain, but rather whether the creative team could find something genuinely new to say within its gloriously tawdry framework.

The answer, delivered with spectacular confidence, is a resounding yes.

Luke Evans Commands the Stage as Frank-N-Furter

Let us begin where any honest review of this show must begin: with Luke Evans in the role of Dr. Frank-N-Furter. The Welsh actor and singer, best known to film audiences from his work in major Hollywood franchises, has been waiting for a role like this his entire career, and he seizes it with both hands and never lets go.

Evans brings a combination of raw vocal power, physical magnetism, and surprising emotional depth to a character that can easily tip into mere caricature. His "Sweet Transvestite" is a star-making Broadway entrance, delivered with the kind of assured command that fills a theater from the first note to the last. He struts, preens, and commands the stage like a man who has been born to wear a corset and six-inch heels, but beneath the glitter and bravado, Evans finds genuine pathos in Frank's desperate need for admiration and connection.

His rendition of "I'm Going Home" in the show's final act is genuinely moving, a quiet moment of vulnerability that lands with unexpected emotional weight. This is not simply a campy villain for audiences to laugh at. Evans makes Frank someone the audience simultaneously fears, desires, and ultimately mourns.

A Vocal Performance for the Ages

It should be noted that Evans's voice is in extraordinary form. His rock tenor soars through O'Brien's demanding score with apparent ease, finding grit and tenderness in equal measure. The role requires an almost impossible combination of qualities, and Evans delivers each one with precision and passion.

Rachel Dratch Steals Every Scene She Inhabits

If Evans is the heart of the production, then Rachel Dratch as the sardonic Narrator is its wicked, twinkling soul. Best known for her comedy work, Dratch proves here that her gifts extend far beyond any single genre. Her Narrator is a magnificent creation, a figure of knowing irony who addresses the audience with the weary affection of someone who has watched these particular humans make these particular mistakes many, many times before.

Dratch's comic timing is, predictably, impeccable. Her exchanges with audience members who shout the traditional call-and-response lines are handled with lightning wit and improvisational flair. She manages to make each performance feel genuinely spontaneous while clearly maintaining complete control of the theatrical moment. But there are also unexpected layers of melancholy and warmth in her performance that elevate the role beyond mere commentary.

Her physical comedy, particularly during the show's more chaotic ensemble sequences, is a masterclass in the art of the perfectly placed reaction. Dratch does more with a raised eyebrow than most performers manage with entire soliloquies.

Juliette Lewis Brings Rock Authenticity to Magenta

Juliette Lewis as Magenta is a revelation and a delight. The actress and rock musician brings genuine musical credibility to a role that demands it, and her chemistry with the rest of the ensemble crackles with authentic energy. Her voice has a raw, slightly dangerous quality that perfectly suits Magenta's volatile nature, and her performance of "The Time Warp" alongside the full company is one of the production's most electrifying moments.

Lewis attacks the role with complete physical commitment, moving through the choreography with the loose, assured energy of someone who has spent years performing on rock stages. There is nothing tentative or overly calculated in her work here. She is fully present in every moment, and the performance shines with that presence.

Direction and Design Electrify the Production

The production's creative team has made bold and largely successful choices throughout. The set design embraces the deliberately theatrical artificiality of the material, constructing a castle environment that feels simultaneously campy and genuinely atmospheric. The lighting design is a particular triumph, using color with extraordinary expressiveness to shift the emotional temperature of scenes in an instant.

The choreography honors the iconic movements audiences expect while finding fresh angles and surprising moments of genuine innovation. The ensemble cast performs with tremendous precision and visible joy, creating the sense of a company that genuinely loves what they are doing.

  • Musical Direction: The band is tight and energetic throughout, finding the perfect balance between rock rawness and theatrical precision.
  • Costume Design: Every sequin, every corset, every pair of platform boots has been chosen with evident care and wit.
  • Sound Design: The production achieves the difficult task of making a large Broadway house feel intimate and immediate during quieter moments.
  • Ensemble Performance: The supporting cast brings remarkable energy and commitment to every number.

The Supporting Cast Rounds Out a Magnificent Production

The performances of the actors playing Brad and Janet deserve significant credit for the production's success. These characters can easily become passive ciphers through whom the audience experiences the story, but in this production they are fully realized individuals with genuine arcs. The audience is invited to track their transformation with real investment, and the performances support that investment admirably.

This is a production that understands the material's capacity to simultaneously mock and celebrate its audience's most secret desires, and it executes that understanding with theatrical mastery.

The actor playing Riff Raff brings a genuinely unsettling physicality to the role, moving through the castle's corridors with a lurching, angular quality that feels both comic and genuinely threatening. Columbia is performed with enormous charm and a vocal style that honors the character's rock and roll roots.

What This Revival Means for Broadway in 2026

Beyond its immediate pleasures, this production of The Rocky Horror Show arrives at a culturally significant moment. Broadway continues its post-pandemic recovery, rebuilding its audience base and reestablishing its identity as a destination for world-class theatrical entertainment. A revival of this particular show, with this particular cast, sends a clear and welcome message: Broadway remains a place where genuine risk-taking is rewarded, where performers of the highest caliber gather to do extraordinary work, and where audiences can experience something that no streaming service or home entertainment system can replicate.

The production's embrace of its own glorious excess is also a statement of theatrical confidence. In an era of endless franchise reboots and hedged creative bets, here is a show that knows exactly what it is and refuses to apologize for a single sequin. That confidence is infectious, and it runs from the creative team through the cast and ultimately into the audience, which leaves the theater buzzing with the particular electric joy that only live performance can generate.

Final Verdict: Do Not Miss This Time Warp

There will be those who wonder whether a show so deeply embedded in cult culture can speak to newcomers who arrive without years of devoted fandom behind them. The answer this production provides is entirely reassuring. The show's themes of self-expression, liberation, and the search for belonging are as resonant as ever, and the storytelling is sharp enough to welcome new audiences while delighting those who know every lyric and every callback.

The Rocky Horror Show on Broadway in 2026 is exactly what theater should be: alive, committed, funny, moving, and completely, unapologetically itself. Luke Evans, Rachel Dratch, and Juliette Lewis lead a production that honors the legacy of O'Brien's brilliant creation while finding something fresh and vital to say about why this particular story has captivated audiences for more than five decades.

Do not miss it. Do the Time Warp again.
