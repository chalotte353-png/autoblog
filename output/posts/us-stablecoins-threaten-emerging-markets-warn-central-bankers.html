US Stablecoins Threaten Emerging Markets, Warn Central Bankers

Central bankers from emerging economies are raising urgent alarms about the rapid expansion of US dollar-pegged stablecoins, warning that their proliferation under the Trump administration poses serious threats to monetary sovereignty and financial stability. Officials argue that the unchecked growth of these crypto assets risks deepening dollarisation across vulnerable economies while opening dangerous new corridors for criminal financial activity.

Investment Reporter
· April 21, 2026 · 5 min read

Central Banks Sound the Alarm Over Dollar-Pegged Crypto Expansion

Senior central bankers and financial regulators from across the developing world are voicing growing concern that the accelerating growth of US dollar-pegged stablecoins represents one of the most significant threats to monetary sovereignty and financial stability in a generation. Their warnings come as the Trump Administration moves to embrace and actively promote the stablecoin industry as a strategic tool for extending dollar dominance globally.

Speaking at a series of high-level meetings held this week, policymakers from Latin America, sub-Saharan Africa, and Southeast Asia said the rapid expansion of stablecoin ecosystems was creating conditions that could fundamentally undermine their ability to conduct independent monetary policy, manage currency reserves, and prevent illicit financial flows within their borders.

The Dollarisation Threat

At the core of central bankers' concerns is the risk of accelerated dollarisation, a process by which citizens and businesses in emerging economies increasingly conduct transactions and store value in US dollars rather than local currencies. Historically, this phenomenon has been driven by inflation, political instability, and loss of public confidence in domestic monetary institutions. The advent of easily accessible, smartphone-based Stablecoins Threatens to dramatically speed up this process even in relatively stable economies.

Economists warn that when a population shifts en masse toward dollar-denominated digital assets, the consequences for local monetary policy can be severe. Central banks lose their ability to influence domestic interest rates effectively, governments face diminished seigniorage revenue, and local banking systems can be starved of the deposit base needed to fund lending and investment.

"We are not opposed to innovation, but what we are seeing is effectively the dollarisation of our retail payment systems through the back door. This is not a theoretical concern. We are already observing behavioral changes among our populations in border regions and in communities with high smartphone penetration," said one senior monetary official from a major Latin American economy, speaking on condition of anonymity.

The concern is particularly acute in nations that have experienced currency crises in recent years, where populations have already demonstrated a strong preference for dollar-denominated assets. In these environments, the low friction and accessibility of stablecoins could trigger rapid and potentially irreversible shifts in monetary behavior.

Trump Administration's Pro-Stablecoin Stance Raises Stakes

The issue has taken on new urgency since the Trump Administration signaled its strong support for US-issued stablecoins as a mechanism for reinforcing dollar hegemony in the digital economy. Administration officials have publicly argued that dollar-backed stablecoins represent a natural extension of American financial power and should be encouraged to proliferate globally.

Legislation currently advancing through Congress would create a formal regulatory framework for stablecoin issuers, granting them a degree of legitimacy and institutional support that could further accelerate adoption. Industry leaders and Treasury officials have both emphasized the geopolitical benefits of ensuring that global crypto transactions remain anchored to the US dollar rather than rival currencies or alternative digital assets.

For emerging market central banks, however, this framing reveals precisely what they fear. What Washington presents as a competitive advantage for American financial interests translates directly into a loss of monetary autonomy for countries on the receiving end of stablecoin adoption.

Criminal Misuse and Regulatory Gaps

Beyond the macroeconomic risks, central bankers and financial intelligence officials are raising serious concerns about the potential for dollar-pegged stablecoins to enable criminal activity at scale. Many stablecoin transactions occur through decentralized or lightly regulated platforms that lack the know-your-customer and anti-money-laundering controls required of traditional financial institutions.

Officials from several countries pointed to concrete examples of stablecoins being used to facilitate:

  • Cross-border capital flight that circumvents domestic currency controls
  • Sanctions evasion by individuals and entities subject to international restrictions
  • Payments linked to drug trafficking and organized crime networks
  • Tax evasion through unreported foreign currency holdings
  • Fraudulent investment schemes targeting retail investors with limited digital literacy

The decentralized nature of many stablecoin platforms makes traditional law enforcement responses extremely difficult. Unlike bank transfers or credit card transactions, stablecoin movements can be structured to obscure beneficial ownership, exploit jurisdictional gaps, and move at speeds that outpace regulatory response capabilities.

The Challenge of Regulatory Arbitrage

A particularly troubling aspect of the situation is the regulatory arbitrage that stablecoin operators can exploit. When the United States establishes a permissive regulatory framework for stablecoin issuers, those entities gain a veneer of legitimacy even when operating in jurisdictions with stricter rules. Emerging market regulators find themselves in the difficult position of trying to restrict products that carry an implicit stamp of approval from the world's largest and most influential financial regulator.

"We can issue warnings and guidance, but when a product is issued and regulated under US law, it carries credibility that is very difficult for us to counter with our own populations," one regulatory official from a Southeast Asian nation explained. "People assume that if it is legal in America, it must be safe and legitimate."

International Bodies Called to Act

In response to mounting pressure from emerging market members, several international financial institutions and multilateral organizations are being urged to develop coordinated frameworks for managing the cross-border risks of stablecoins. The Bank for International Settlements and the Financial Stability Board have both flagged stablecoin proliferation as a systemic risk requiring international coordination, but critics argue that existing proposals lack the enforcement mechanisms needed to be effective.

Some officials are calling for the International Monetary Fund to formally incorporate stablecoin-driven dollarisation risks into its Article IV surveillance processes, giving the institution a mandate to flag cases where crypto adoption is materially affecting monetary sovereignty in member states.

Others are pushing for bilateral agreements that would require US stablecoin issuers to comply with the regulatory requirements of jurisdictions where their products are actively marketed and used, rather than relying solely on home country supervision.

Domestic Digital Currency as a Counter-Strategy

Several central banks are accelerating the development and deployment of their own central bank digital currencies as a defensive measure against stablecoin encroachment. By offering populations a regulated, locally issued digital currency with comparable convenience to stablecoins, monetary authorities hope to provide an alternative that preserves domestic monetary sovereignty while meeting modern payment expectations.

However, experts caution that central bank digital currencies face significant challenges in competing with the established network effects and global liquidity of dollar-pegged stablecoins, particularly in economies where trust in government institutions remains fragile.

Industry Pushback

Stablecoin issuers and their advocates in Washington dispute the framing offered by emerging market central bankers. Industry representatives argue that stablecoins expand financial access for unbanked and underbanked populations, reduce remittance costs for migrant workers sending money home, and provide a safe harbor for ordinary citizens in countries where local currency instability destroys savings.

They further contend that the solution to criminal misuse lies not in restricting stablecoin adoption but in building better international regulatory cooperation and improving the on-chain analytics tools available to law enforcement agencies.

The debate is unlikely to be resolved quickly. As stablecoins continue to grow in scale and sophistication, the tension between Washington's enthusiasm for dollar-pegged digital assets and the concerns of emerging market central banks looks set to become one of the defining fault lines in international financial diplomacy for years to come.
