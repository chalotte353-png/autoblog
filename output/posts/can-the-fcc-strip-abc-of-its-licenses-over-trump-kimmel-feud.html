Can the FCC Strip ABC of Its Licenses Over Trump-Kimmel Feud?

The ongoing feud between former President Donald Trump and late-night host Jimmy Kimmel has escalated into questions about whether federal regulators could revoke ABC's broadcast licenses. While the Federal Communications Commission does hold the authority to pull licenses, legal experts say the path to forcing ABC off the air would be extraordinarily difficult. The clash has reignited a broader debate about free speech, regulatory overreach, and the independence of broadcast media in America.

The Trump-Kimmel Conflict and Federal Regulatory Power

What began as a war of words between Donald Trump and late-night television host Jimmy Kimmel has evolved into a serious policy question that reaches into the heart of American broadcast law. As of May 2026, political observers, media attorneys, and First Amendment scholars are all asking the same question: could the Federal Communications Commission actually revoke ABC's broadcast licenses in response to political pressure from the White House?

The answer, according to a broad range of legal experts, is technically yes but practically and legally, extraordinarily difficult to execute without setting off a constitutional firestorm.

How Broadcast Licensing Actually Works

To understand the controversy, it is important to first understand how broadcast licensing functions in the United States. Television and radio stations that use public airwaves are required by law to obtain licenses from the FCC, which is an independent federal agency. These licenses must be renewed periodically, and the FCC is empowered to revoke them if a licensee is found to have violated federal communications laws or acted against the public interest.

ABC, one of the major broadcast networks in the country, does not hold a single national license. Instead, its local affiliate stations each hold individual licenses granted by the FCC. The network itself operates through these affiliates, many of which are owned and operated directly by Disney, ABC's parent company.

This structure is critical. Any regulatory action targeting ABC would technically need to be directed at individual station licenses across the country, not at the network as a whole. That significantly raises the logistical and legal bar for any effort to silence a broadcaster.

What Legal Experts Are Saying

Constitutional attorneys and media law specialists have been nearly unanimous in warning that any attempt by the FCC to revoke ABC's licenses solely because of political programming content would face immediate and severe legal challenges.

"The First Amendment does not evaporate simply because a broadcaster uses public airwaves. Courts have consistently held that content-based government actions against broadcasters require an extraordinarily compelling justification," said one prominent First Amendment attorney familiar with FCC proceedings.

The legal framework protecting broadcasters is layered. While the Supreme Court's landmark 1969 ruling in Red Lion Broadcasting Co. v. FCC gave the government broader latitude to regulate broadcast content compared to print media, courts have consistently struck down attempts to weaponize regulatory agencies for political retaliation against specific media outlets.

Key legal obstacles include the following:

  • First Amendment protections: Content-based government retaliation against a broadcaster would almost certainly be challenged as a violation of free speech and freedom of the press.
  • The public interest standard: The FCC can only revoke licenses based on violations of law or failure to serve the public interest. Political satire and commentary on a late-night talk show would not traditionally meet that threshold.
  • Due process requirements: License revocation requires a formal hearing process, giving the licensee the opportunity to respond and appeal, which can take years to resolve through the courts.
  • Independence of the FCC: The FCC is structured as an independent agency, meaning a president cannot simply direct it to take punitive action against political opponents without raising serious separation of powers concerns.

The Political Dimension of the Dispute

The feud between Trump and Kimmel is not new, but it has sharpened significantly in recent months. Trump has repeatedly used his social media platform and public statements to accuse Kimmel and ABC of bias, unfair treatment, and what he has described as politically motivated attacks against him and his administration. Trump has also floated the idea of using regulatory pressure as a response to media coverage he finds objectionable.

Critics of Trump have characterized these statements as authoritarian in nature, warning that even the suggestion of using federal regulatory power to punish a broadcaster sets a dangerous precedent for press freedom in the United States.

Supporters of the president, however, argue that broadcast networks enjoy enormous public benefits through their use of government-licensed airwaves and should be held accountable for content that crosses into what they describe as political propaganda.

Historical Precedents and Why They Matter

American history offers cautionary examples of what happens when political pressure intersects with broadcast regulation. The FCC's Fairness Doctrine, which required broadcasters to present contrasting viewpoints on controversial issues, was abolished in 1987 amid concerns that it actually chilled free speech rather than promoting it. Since then, the regulatory environment has moved decisively toward protecting broadcaster editorial independence.

There have been isolated cases where the FCC has revoked broadcast licenses, but these have involved clear legal violations such as fraudulent misrepresentation during license applications, failure to operate in the public interest in demonstrable ways, or consistent broadcast of indecent material. No license has ever been successfully revoked solely on the basis of a broadcaster's political commentary or satire.

Legal scholars point to this history as evidence that any attempt to pursue ABC's licenses on political grounds would represent a dramatic and unprecedented departure from established regulatory norms.

The Broader Threat to Media Independence

Beyond the specific legal questions, media observers are raising alarms about what the public discussion of license revocation signals for the health of the American press. Even if regulatory action never materializes, the mere threat of government interference can create a chilling effect on broadcasters, encouraging self-censorship and editorial caution.

This phenomenon, sometimes called the "regulatory chill," is considered by press freedom advocates to be among the most serious indirect threats to journalism in a democracy.

"You do not need to actually pull a license to achieve a silencing effect. The threat alone can be enough to make newsrooms and entertainment producers think twice about what they say," noted one media policy researcher at a major American university.

Organizations dedicated to protecting press freedom have already begun issuing statements condemning what they describe as politically motivated pressure on broadcast regulators, urging the FCC to maintain its independence from executive branch influence.

What Would Actually Need to Happen for Licenses to Be Revoked

For the FCC to successfully revoke licenses belonging to ABC-owned stations, regulators would need to initiate formal proceedings, gather evidence of specific legal violations, provide the licensee with notice and an opportunity for a hearing, and withstand extensive judicial review. The entire process could take years and would almost certainly end up before federal appeals courts and potentially the Supreme Court.

Even FCC commissioners who might be sympathetic to political pressure would be constrained by the agency's own procedural rules and the constitutional framework within which it operates. Legal experts broadly agree that a license revocation motivated by political animus rather than legitimate public interest violations would be thrown out by the courts at the earliest available opportunity.

The FCC's Current Composition and Political Leanings

The composition of the FCC itself plays a role in how seriously any license threat should be taken. As of 2026, the commission's makeup reflects appointments made during the Trump Administration, which could theoretically make it more receptive to taking action that aligns with the president's preferences. However, even commissioners sympathetic to the administration have shown caution about actions that could expose the agency to legal reversal and damage its institutional credibility.

The agency operates under congressional oversight and must answer to federal courts. Commissioners are aware that overreach in this area would invite legislative response and judicial rebuke, outcomes that would ultimately weaken rather than strengthen the regulatory authority of the FCC.

Conclusion: A Tough Road Ahead for Any License Action

The question of whether the FCC can yank ABC's licenses amid the Trump-Kimmel dispute has a straightforward legal answer: while the mechanism theoretically exists, using it for political purposes would face overwhelming legal resistance. The First Amendment, decades of regulatory precedent, procedural requirements, and the independent structure of the FCC itself all combine to make any such effort extraordinarily unlikely to succeed.

What the controversy does illuminate is the ongoing tension between political power and press freedom in America, a tension that shows no signs of easing as the 2026 political environment remains deeply polarized. Whether or not formal action is ever taken, the debate itself serves as a reminder of how fragile the norms protecting media independence can become when subjected to sustained political pressure.

For now, legal experts advise watching the FCC's formal actions, rather than political rhetoric, as the true measure of how far regulators are actually willing to go in the dispute between the White House and one of America's most prominent broadcast networks.
