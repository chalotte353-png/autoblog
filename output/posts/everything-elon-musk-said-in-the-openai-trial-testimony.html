Some of it may come back to haunt him.

Elon Musk Takes the Stand in the OpenAI Trial

May 03, 2026 — The courtroom drama surrounding the OpenAI trial reached a fever pitch this week as Elon Musk, one of the company's original co-founders, stepped into the witness box to deliver testimony that observers described as volatile, lengthy, and at times deeply contradictory. The Tesla and SpaceX chief executive had waited months for this moment, and when it finally arrived, he did not disappoint anyone looking for spectacle.

The trial, which centers on whether OpenAI and its leadership violated the founding charter of the nonprofit organization by shifting toward a for-profit model, has drawn enormous attention from the technology industry, legal scholars, and AI researchers worldwide. Musk, who filed the original lawsuit claiming that OpenAI and its chief executive Sam Altman betrayed the organization's founding mission to develop artificial intelligence for the benefit of humanity, used his time on the stand to lay out his version of events in exhaustive detail.

The Founding Story, According to Musk

A significant portion of Musk's testimony focused on what he described as the original intent behind OpenAI's founding in 2015. Musk insisted that the organization was always meant to be a nonprofit research lab that would counterbalance the perceived dominance of companies like Google in the AI space. He claimed that the entire premise of his involvement and his financial contributions was rooted in the belief that OpenAI would remain open, transparent, and mission-driven.

"The whole point was that this would not be a for-profit company beholden to investors. That was the deal. That was why I got involved and why I gave money. What happened to OpenAI is a complete betrayal of everything we agreed to."

Opposing counsel pushed back aggressively on this characterization, presenting internal emails and documents suggesting that the transition away from a strict nonprofit structure had been discussed as early as 2017, well before Musk formally departed from OpenAI's board in 2018. Musk acknowledged the existence of some of these communications but disputed their interpretation, arguing that exploratory conversations about structure never amounted to an agreement to abandon the nonprofit mission.

The Departure From the Board

One of the most closely watched portions of the testimony concerned the circumstances under which Musk left OpenAI's board. Musk has previously claimed that he resigned because of a conflict of interest related to Tesla's own artificial intelligence ambitions. During his testimony, however, he offered a somewhat different account, suggesting that tensions with Sam Altman and other board members over the direction of the organization had become irreconcilable.

He testified that he had proposed taking over as chief executive of OpenAI at one point, believing that stronger leadership was needed to keep the organization on mission. When that proposal was rejected, he said, he felt there was no productive path forward for him to remain on the board. Defense attorneys quickly seized on this admission, suggesting it painted a picture of a businessman motivated in part by wounded pride and a desire for control rather than purely principled concerns about the nonprofit mission.

Questions About xAI and Competitive Motivations

Defense attorneys devoted considerable time to questioning Musk about his founding of xAI, his own artificial intelligence company, which launched after he left OpenAI's board and has since developed its own large language model known as Grok. The line of questioning was clearly designed to suggest that Musk's lawsuit was motivated not by genuine concern over OpenAI's charitable mission but by competitive business interests.

Musk denied this framing categorically. He insisted that xAI was founded precisely because he had become disillusioned with what OpenAI had become, and that his decision to create a competing company was a response to OpenAI's failures rather than a cause of his legal complaints. Still, the optics were uncomfortable, and legal analysts watching the proceedings noted that the exchange visibly rattled the billionaire in a way that earlier questioning had not.

The Microsoft Relationship Under Scrutiny

Another central theme of Musk's testimony was his characterization of OpenAI's deepening relationship with Microsoft, which has invested billions of dollars in the company over multiple rounds of funding. Musk argued that the partnership represented the ultimate corruption of OpenAI's founding principles, effectively turning what was supposed to be a public benefit organization into a proprietary technology asset controlled by one of the world's largest corporations.

  • Musk claimed that the Microsoft investment fundamentally changed OpenAI's incentive structure, prioritizing commercial returns over safety research.
  • He argued that key decisions about GPT model releases and deployment were being driven by revenue considerations rather than safety assessments.
  • He testified that internal communications he had reviewed suggested that concerns raised by researchers about model risks were sometimes deprioritized when they conflicted with product launch timelines.
  • He expressed concern that the concentration of advanced AI capability within a small number of well-funded corporate entities posed systemic risks to society.

OpenAI's defense team disputed each of these claims, presenting their own witnesses and documents in subsequent sessions to argue that the company's safety practices have become more rigorous over time, not less, and that the nonprofit board retains meaningful oversight over the organization's direction.

Moments That May Come Back to Haunt Him

Legal observers and technology reporters covering the trial noted several moments in Musk's testimony that appeared likely to complicate his legal position going forward. Perhaps most notably, Musk was asked directly whether he had ever proposed that OpenAI pursue government contracts for defense-related artificial intelligence applications. After a lengthy pause, he acknowledged that such discussions had occurred during the early days of the organization, a concession that defense attorneys suggested undercut his argument that commercial and governmental applications of AI were always antithetical to OpenAI's mission.

There was also a tense exchange over a series of text messages between Musk and Sam Altman dating from 2017 and 2018. The messages, introduced into evidence by OpenAI's legal team, appeared to show Musk expressing support for the company raising outside capital to fund its research ambitions. Musk disputed the context of those messages, arguing that he had been discussing a very specific and limited funding scenario that differed fundamentally from the broad commercialization that ultimately occurred. Whether the jury found that distinction convincing remained an open question at the close of testimony.

What Comes Next in the Trial

With Musk's testimony now complete, the trial is expected to continue for several more weeks. Sam Altman is anticipated to take the stand in the coming days, and legal experts expect his testimony to directly contradict several of the core claims Musk made under oath. Additional witnesses from OpenAI's research and legal teams are also expected to testify about the internal deliberations that led to the company's for-profit restructuring.

The outcome of the trial carries enormous implications not just for OpenAI and Musk personally, but for the broader question of how artificial intelligence companies are governed and held accountable to stated public interest missions. A ruling in Musk's favor could set a significant legal precedent requiring AI organizations that originated as nonprofits to honor those commitments even as they grow and seek outside investment.

The Bigger Picture for AI Governance

Beyond the personal drama between two of Silicon Valley's most prominent figures, the OpenAI trial has opened a rare public window into the internal culture and decision-making processes of one of the most consequential technology companies on the planet. Regardless of how the jury ultimately rules, the documents and testimony already introduced into the record have reshaped public understanding of how OpenAI evolved from a scrappy nonprofit research lab into a multibillion-dollar enterprise at the center of the global AI revolution.

For Musk, the trial represents both a legal gamble and a reputational wager. He came to the stand with a great deal to say about betrayal, ambition, and the future of artificial intelligence. Whether the evidence supports the story he told, and whether the jury believes it, will determine not just the outcome of this lawsuit but the shape of a legal and regulatory conversation about AI that is only just beginning.
