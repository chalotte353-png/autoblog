ER Visits for Tick Bites Surge in 2026: What Experts Say

Emergency rooms across the country are reporting a significant increase in tick bite cases in 2026, alarming public health officials and physicians alike. Experts point to a combination of environmental shifts, population growth in tick habitats, and heightened public awareness as key drivers. Understanding the risks and knowing how to protect yourself has never been more important.
Anna Kowalski
Europe Correspondent
· May 02, 2026 · 6 min read

A Growing Public Health Concern

Emergency departments across the United States are seeing an unusually high number of patients presenting with tick bites in the spring of 2026, and medical professionals are urging the public to take the threat seriously. While tick season has always carried inherent risks, this year's spike is drawing particular concern from epidemiologists, infectious disease specialists, and primary care physicians who are witnessing the trend firsthand.

From rural communities in New England to suburban neighborhoods in the mid-Atlantic and even parts of the Pacific Northwest, reports of tick encounters and subsequent emergency visits have climbed well above seasonal averages. Health departments in multiple states have already issued advisories, encouraging residents to take precautionary measures before spending time outdoors.

Why Are More People Going to the ER?

Experts point to several interconnected factors that have contributed to this larger-than-usual surge. The phenomenon is not the result of any single cause but rather a convergence of environmental, behavioral, and ecological trends that have been building for several years.

Warmer Winters and Extended Tick Seasons

One of the most significant contributors to the rise in tick encounters is the pattern of warmer winters observed across much of the northern hemisphere. Ticks, particularly the black-legged tick known to transmit Lyme disease, historically experienced die-offs during harsh winters. Milder cold seasons have allowed larger populations of ticks to survive and become active earlier in the year.

Dr. Ellen Marsh, an infectious disease specialist at a leading regional hospital, explains it plainly:

"We used to have a relatively predictable window for tick activity. Now that window has expanded significantly. Ticks are emerging weeks earlier in the spring and remaining active well into late autumn. That means a longer exposure window for everyone going outdoors."

This extended season gives ticks more opportunities to encounter human hosts, and with more people spending time outdoors following the pandemic-era embrace of nature recreation, the chances of a tick bite have increased substantially.

Expanding Tick Habitats

Ticks are not just surviving longer. They are spreading into new geographic areas. Species that were once confined to specific regions are now being found hundreds of miles outside their historical ranges. The lone star tick, for example, has extended its territory northward, while the Asian longhorned tick, an invasive species first detected in the United States less than a decade ago, continues to establish itself in new states.

Suburban sprawl has also played a role. As residential development pushes into previously wooded areas, more people are living in close proximity to deer, mice, and other wildlife that serve as primary hosts for ticks throughout various stages of their life cycle. Backyards, parks, and walking trails in these transitional zones have become prime locations for tick encounters.

Increased Outdoor Recreation

Following several years of increased interest in hiking, camping, gardening, and other outdoor activities, Americans continue to spend more time in tick-prone environments. Many people remain unaware of the risks or do not take adequate precautions when venturing into grassy or wooded areas. This behavioral factor has amplified the impact of the ecological changes already underway.

Greater Awareness and Reporting

It is also worth noting that some of the increase in ER visits reflects heightened public awareness about tick-borne illnesses rather than purely more bites occurring. After years of public health campaigns around Lyme disease and other tick-borne conditions, more people are choosing to seek medical evaluation after discovering a tick, even when the bite may not present immediate symptoms.

While this increased vigilance is generally a positive development, it does place additional strain on emergency departments that are already managing significant patient loads. Medical professionals are encouraging people to understand when a tick bite warrants an ER visit and when it can be addressed through a primary care physician or urgent care clinic instead.

The Range of Tick-Borne Illnesses

Public concern about tick bites extends far beyond Lyme disease, though it remains the most commonly diagnosed tick-borne illness in the United States. Other conditions transmitted by ticks include:

  • Rocky Mountain spotted fever, a potentially life-threatening bacterial infection
  • Anaplasmosis, which can cause fever, headache, and muscle aches
  • Babesiosis, a parasitic infection affecting red blood cells
  • Ehrlichiosis, a bacterial illness with flu-like symptoms
  • Powassan virus, a rare but serious neurological threat with no specific treatment
  • Alpha-gal syndrome, an allergic reaction to red meat triggered by lone star tick bites

The diversity of illnesses that ticks can transmit underscores the importance of prevention and prompt medical attention when a bite is discovered. Some conditions, if left untreated, can lead to serious complications including organ damage and chronic illness.

How to Protect Yourself and Your Family

The good news is that there are practical and highly effective measures that individuals and families can take to significantly reduce their risk of tick bites and the illnesses that can follow them.

Before You Head Outdoors

Apply an EPA-registered insect repellent containing DEET, picaridin, or IR3535 to exposed skin. For clothing, products containing permethrin provide long-lasting protection and can be applied to gear and apparel before use. Wearing long sleeves, long pants tucked into socks, and light-colored clothing makes it easier to spot ticks before they have an opportunity to bite.

During Outdoor Activities

Stick to the center of trails whenever possible and avoid brushing against tall grass, shrubs, or leaf litter, which are common habitats for ticks waiting to latch onto a passing host. Perform periodic checks on yourself and companions, especially around the hairline, behind the ears, under the arms, around the waist, and behind the knees.

After Coming Indoors

Conduct a thorough full-body tick check within two hours of returning home. Shower promptly to wash off unattached ticks. Tumble clothing in a dryer on high heat for at least ten minutes to kill any ticks that may be clinging to fabric. Check pets as well, since dogs and cats can carry ticks inside the home.

If You Find a Tick

Remove attached ticks as quickly as possible using fine-tipped tweezers. Grasp the tick as close to the skin's surface as possible and pull upward with steady, even pressure. Do not twist or jerk the tick, as this can cause parts of its mouth to break off and remain in the skin. Clean the bite area thoroughly with rubbing alcohol or soap and water. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention provides comprehensive guidance on tick removal and when to seek medical care.

"The most important thing is to remove the tick promptly and correctly. The longer a tick remains attached, the higher the risk of disease transmission. Most pathogens require the tick to be attached for a significant period before transmission occurs, which means early removal is a powerful protective measure," says Dr. Marsh.

When to Seek Medical Attention

Not every tick bite requires an emergency room visit, and physicians are encouraging the public to make informed decisions about their level of care. An ER visit is most appropriate when a person experiences severe allergic reaction symptoms, difficulty breathing, significant swelling, or rapid onset of fever and neurological symptoms following a tick bite.

For most tick bite cases, particularly those where the tick has been successfully removed and no immediate symptoms are present, scheduling an appointment with a primary care provider or visiting an urgent care clinic is the more appropriate course of action. Physicians may recommend a single preventive dose of doxycycline in certain circumstances to reduce the risk of Lyme disease.

A Community and Policy Response

Public health departments at the local, state, and federal levels are responding to the uptick in cases with expanded tick surveillance programs, updated public education campaigns, and increased funding for research into tick-borne disease prevention and treatment. Several municipalities are also exploring environmental management strategies, including targeted acaricide applications and habitat modifications, to reduce tick populations in high-risk public spaces.

Researchers are also making progress on Lyme disease vaccine development, with several candidates in advanced clinical trial stages as of 2026. These developments offer hope for a more permanent solution to one of the most common and debilitating tick-borne conditions in the coming years.

The Bottom Line

The surge in emergency room visits for tick bites in 2026 is a stark reminder that tick-borne illness is a growing public health challenge that demands attention from individuals, communities, and policymakers alike. While the factors driving the increase are complex, the tools for personal protection are straightforward and widely accessible. Taking a few extra minutes to prepare before heading outdoors, performing thorough tick checks after returning home, and knowing when and how to seek appropriate medical care can make a meaningful difference in keeping yourself and your loved ones safe throughout the season and beyond.
