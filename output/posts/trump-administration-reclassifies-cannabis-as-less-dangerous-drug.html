Trump Administration Reclassifies Cannabis as Less Dangerous Drug

The Trump administration has officially reclassified cannabis from a Schedule I to a Schedule III controlled substance, marking one of the most significant shifts in American drug policy in decades. The move is intended to accelerate medical research and expand patient access to marijuana-based treatments. Health advocates and lawmakers across the political spectrum are calling the decision a historic turning point in how the United States approaches cannabis regulation.

Political Editor

A Landmark Shift in United States Drug Policy

In a move that has reshaped the landscape of American drug regulation, the Trump Administration formally reclassified cannabis from a Schedule I controlled substance to a Schedule III controlled substance on April 24, 2026. The decision, announced by the Drug Enforcement Administration in coordination with the Department of Health and Human Services, marks the most consequential change to federal marijuana policy in more than fifty years.

Schedule I substances are defined as drugs with no accepted medical use and a high potential for abuse, placing cannabis alongside substances such as heroin under previous federal classifications. Schedule III drugs, by contrast, are recognized as having moderate to low potential for dependence and accepted medical applications. The reclassification effectively acknowledges what millions of patients, doctors, and researchers have long argued: that cannabis carries significant therapeutic value and does not belong in the same federal category as the most dangerous controlled substances.

The formal rule change was published in the Federal Register on Thursday morning, triggering an immediate response from lawmakers, medical professionals, advocacy organizations, and the Cannabis Industry, all of whom had been anticipating the announcement following months of regulatory review and public comment periods.

What Prompted the Reclassification

The reclassification did not emerge overnight. The process was set in motion under the previous administration when the Department of Health and Human Services recommended in 2023 that cannabis be moved to Schedule III following a comprehensive scientific review. The Biden administration had initiated the formal rulemaking process, but the Trump Administration, upon returning to the White House in January 2025, chose to advance and finalize the rule rather than reverse course.

Officials within the administration cited a growing body of scientific evidence supporting the medical benefits of cannabis, particularly in the treatment of chronic pain, epilepsy, anxiety disorders, and nausea associated with chemotherapy. The decision also reflected pressure from within the Republican Party, where a growing contingent of lawmakers and voters have embraced a more pragmatic stance on marijuana policy.

"This reclassification is a recognition of science and of the reality that millions of Americans rely on medical cannabis as a legitimate treatment option. It opens the door to the kind of rigorous research we need to fully understand both its benefits and its risks," said a senior administration health official at a press briefing Thursday morning.

The administration was also responding to significant economic and legal inconsistencies created by the gap between federal law and state law. As of early 2026, forty-four states and the District of Columbia have legalized some form of Medical Marijuana, and twenty-four states have legalized recreational use. The Schedule I classification had long created a patchwork of legal complications for cannabis businesses, banks, and healthcare providers operating within state-legal frameworks.

Impact on Medical Research

Perhaps the most immediate and widely celebrated consequence of the reclassification is the anticipated expansion of cannabis research. Under Schedule I classification, researchers faced extraordinary barriers to studying cannabis, including strict licensing requirements, limited access to research-grade marijuana supplies, and significant bureaucratic hurdles that discouraged academic and pharmaceutical institutions from pursuing cannabis-related studies.

With cannabis now classified under Schedule III, those restrictions are substantially reduced. Universities, hospitals, and private research institutions will find it far easier to obtain funding, institutional approval, and access to cannabis for clinical trials and observational studies.

Key Research Areas Expected to Benefit

  • Chronic pain management: Researchers can now more rigorously study cannabis as an alternative to opioid-based painkillers, which remain a significant driver of addiction and overdose deaths across the country.
  • Neurological conditions: Studies on cannabis-derived treatments for conditions such as multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's disease, and traumatic brain injury will face fewer regulatory obstacles.
  • Mental health disorders: Clinical trials exploring the relationship between cannabis compounds and conditions including PTSD, anxiety, and depression can now proceed with greater institutional support.
  • Pediatric epilepsy: Building on the success of FDA-approved cannabidiol medications, researchers can investigate additional cannabis-based treatments for children with treatment-resistant seizure disorders.
  • Oncology: The role of cannabis in managing cancer-related symptoms, including pain, appetite loss, and treatment-induced nausea, can be studied under more rigorous scientific protocols.

Medical associations that had previously called for expanded research access welcomed the change, noting that the lack of robust clinical data had long hampered physicians trying to make evidence-based recommendations to patients inquiring about cannabis as a treatment option.

What the Reclassification Does Not Do

While the reclassification represents a major regulatory milestone, it is important to understand its limitations. Cannabis remains a federally controlled substance under Schedule III, meaning it is still illegal under federal law for recreational use. The decision does not legalize marijuana at the federal level, nor does it immediately resolve all of the legal conflicts between state and federal law that have created complications for cannabis businesses over the past decade.

Banks and financial institutions, for example, will still face some degree of uncertainty in providing services to cannabis companies, though advocates expect the reclassification to ease some of those concerns over time. The administration has indicated that additional regulatory guidance addressing financial services and interstate commerce issues may follow, but no firm timeline has been announced.

Furthermore, the reclassification does not automatically expunge the records of individuals convicted of cannabis-related offenses under prior federal law. Criminal justice reform advocates have called on Congress and the administration to take additional steps to address that dimension of the issue.

Reactions from Across the Political Spectrum

The announcement drew a broad and varied response. Many Republican lawmakers who had previously been skeptical of cannabis reform offered cautious support, framing the reclassification as a sensible deregulatory measure grounded in science and states' rights. Democrats largely praised the move while emphasizing that it did not go far enough and renewed calls for full federal legalization.

The Cannabis Industry responded with enthusiasm, with shares in publicly traded cannabis companies rising sharply in early trading on Thursday. Industry leaders described the reclassification as a turning point that could ultimately lead to a more normalized and transparent regulatory environment for cannabis businesses.

"For too long, the Schedule I status created an impossible situation for companies trying to operate legally within their states while navigating an inconsistent and punishing federal framework. Today's decision is a critical first step toward a rational national cannabis policy," said the chief executive of one of the country's largest licensed cannabis operators.

Patient advocacy groups were equally positive, highlighting that the reclassification would reduce the stigma many patients feel when discussing cannabis with their doctors and potentially expand insurance coverage options for cannabis-based therapies in the future.

A Turning Point With More Work Ahead

The reclassification of cannabis to Schedule III is widely viewed as a watershed moment in the decades-long effort to align federal drug policy with scientific evidence and public opinion. Polls consistently show that a strong majority of Americans support some form of marijuana legalization, and support for medical cannabis access has reached broad consensus across age groups, regions, and political affiliations.

Nevertheless, significant policy work remains. The administration will need to develop comprehensive regulatory frameworks governing how cannabis is produced, tested, marketed, and prescribed within the Schedule III structure. The Food and Drug Administration is expected to play a central role in shaping those standards in the months and years ahead.

For now, patients, researchers, healthcare providers, and industry stakeholders are absorbing the significance of a change that, while incremental in some respects, fundamentally alters the federal government's relationship with one of the most widely used and debated substances in American life. The long road toward a coherent, evidence-based national cannabis policy has taken a decisive step forward.
