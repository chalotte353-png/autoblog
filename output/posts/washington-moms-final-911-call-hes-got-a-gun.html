Washington Mom's Final 911 Call: 'He's Got a Gun!'

A Washington state mother made desperate pleas to 911 dispatchers four times in just 48 hours, warning that her ex-boyfriend was stalking her. Her final call captured the terrifying moment he ran her off the road and opened fire on her truck, raising urgent questions about how the system failed to protect her.
Elena Vasquez
World Affairs Editor
· May 04, 2026 · 5 min read

A Mother's Desperate Calls for Help

In the final days of her life, Gloria Choi, a Washington state mother, reached out to Lakewood emergency dispatchers not once, but four times in just 48 hours. She was terrified. Her ex-boyfriend, she told operators, was following her, watching her, and she feared for her life. Those calls, officials say, were a clear and urgent cry for intervention. What happened next has left a community shaken and a family shattered.

On May 4, 2026, details of Choi's harrowing final moments continue to emerge, drawing widespread outrage and reigniting a national conversation about how law enforcement agencies respond to stalking complaints and domestic violence warnings before tragedy strikes.

Four Calls in 48 Hours

According to authorities and family members, Gloria Choi and her close friends contacted the Lakewood, Washington, 911 dispatch center multiple times over a two-day period leading up to her death. Each call described escalating behavior from her ex-boyfriend, who she alleged was surveilling her movements, showing up at locations she frequented, and making her feel physically unsafe.

Advocates for domestic violence victims note that this pattern of repeated 911 contact is tragically common among women who are being stalked by intimate partners. Despite the volume and urgency of the calls, Choi remained vulnerable on the streets of Lakewood.

"She called for help. She called again and again. She did everything she was supposed to do, and the system still could not save her."

Those words, spoken by a family representative in the days following Choi's death, capture the grief and fury felt by those who knew her. Friends described her as a devoted mother, a warm and generous person who was trying to rebuild her life after a difficult relationship.

The Horrific Final Moments

Two days after the initial stalking complaints were filed, Choi's worst fears became reality. While driving her truck through Lakewood, her ex-boyfriend allegedly pursued her vehicle before running her off the road. As her truck came to a stop, he opened fire, riddling the vehicle with bullets.

In what investigators describe as one of the most chilling 911 recordings in recent local memory, Choi was actively connected to a dispatcher when the attack unfolded. Her voice, audibly panicked, can be heard shouting, "He's got a gun!" before the shooting began. The dispatcher remained on the line as the terrifying scene played out in real time.

Emergency services were dispatched immediately, but by the time first responders arrived at the scene, the damage had been done. Gloria Choi did not survive the attack.

Community Reaction and Demands for Accountability

News of Choi's death and the circumstances surrounding it spread rapidly across Washington state and beyond. Vigils were held in Lakewood, where community members gathered to mourn and to demand answers from local officials about why more was not done to protect her during those critical 48 hours.

Local advocacy organizations focused on domestic violence and stalking prevention have called for an independent review of how the Lakewood Police Department and 911 dispatch center handled Choi's repeated complaints. Questions are being raised about whether proper protocols were followed, whether a protective order was pursued, and whether adequate resources were deployed to keep Choi safe.

What Stalking Laws Say

Under Washington state law, stalking is classified as a serious criminal offense. The law defines stalking as a course of conduct directed at a specific person that would cause a reasonable person to feel fear. When the stalking involves a current or former intimate partner, the offense can carry enhanced penalties.

Legal experts point out that despite these protections existing on paper, enforcement remains inconsistent. Victims are frequently told to seek civil protective orders on their own, a process that can take days or weeks, leaving them dangerously exposed in the interim.

  • Stalking is a felony in Washington state when it involves threats or prior criminal history with the victim.
  • Protective orders can be sought through civil court but require time, documentation, and in many cases legal assistance.
  • Law enforcement response to stalking complaints can vary widely depending on jurisdiction, staffing, and departmental training.
  • Studies show that the period immediately following a breakup is statistically the most dangerous time for victims of intimate partner violence.

The Systemic Failure Behind Individual Tragedies

Gloria Choi's case is not an isolated incident. Across the United States, thousands of women report stalking behavior to law enforcement each year, only to face bureaucratic delays, undertrained responders, or inadequate follow-through. Researchers and advocates have long argued that the gap between reporting and meaningful intervention is where lives are lost.

A report from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention found that approximately one in six women in the United States has experienced stalking victimization at some point in her lifetime. The report also notes that the majority of stalking victims know their perpetrator, with a significant portion being current or former intimate partners.

In Choi's case, the alleged perpetrator was known. The threat was documented. The fear was expressed repeatedly and on record. Yet the outcome was catastrophic.

What Could Have Been Done Differently

Experts in domestic violence intervention have begun to publicly analyze the Choi case as a study in systemic shortfalls. Among the responses they say could have made a difference are the following measures that were available but, apparently, not fully deployed:

  • An immediate welfare check and formal criminal stalking investigation following the first 911 call.
  • Coordination between dispatch, patrol officers, and a domestic violence liaison to ensure continuity across multiple complaints.
  • Proactive outreach to Choi about emergency protective order options available through the court system.
  • Arrest of the alleged perpetrator if probable cause for stalking charges was established based on her documented complaints.
  • A safety plan developed in partnership with a victim advocate who could have provided around-the-clock support.

None of these steps, according to available information, appear to have been taken in the time between Choi's first call and her murder.

A Family Left Behind

Beyond the policy debates and systemic questions lies the deeply personal devastation felt by Gloria Choi's family. She was a mother, a daughter, a friend. Those who loved her are now left to navigate grief while simultaneously fighting for answers and accountability.

Her family has indicated they intend to pursue legal action and have hired an attorney to investigate the city of Lakewood's handling of her stalking complaints. They want to know precisely what steps, if any, were taken after each of her four calls and why the response was not more aggressive given the documented pattern of escalating threats.

"She trusted that help was coming. She kept calling because she believed someone would stop him. That trust was broken, and it cost her everything."

Calls for Reform

Washington state legislators have been urged by advocacy groups to revisit the state's stalking statutes and law enforcement response protocols in light of Gloria Choi's death. Proposals being discussed include mandatory escalation procedures when a victim contacts 911 multiple times within a defined window, automatic notification of a domestic violence advocate upon the second call, and streamlined emergency protective order processes that do not require victims to navigate the court system alone.

For now, a community mourns, a family grieves, and a 911 recording stands as a devastating reminder of what happens when the system moves too slowly for the danger at hand. Gloria Choi called for help. She called again and again. The final recording of her voice, crying out in fear on that last desperate call, is a sound that those who have heard it say they will never forget.

As investigations continue and public pressure mounts, advocates say that meaningful reform must follow. Without it, Gloria Choi's death risks becoming one more entry in a long and painful list of preventable tragedies.
