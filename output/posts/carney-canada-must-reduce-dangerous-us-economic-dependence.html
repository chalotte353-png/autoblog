Carney: Canada Must Reduce Dangerous US Economic Dependence

Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney has issued a stark warning that his country's deep economic dependence on the United States represents a structural weakness that must be urgently corrected. Speaking in a nationally broadcast video address, Carney called for a fundamental reorientation of Canada's trade and economic partnerships. The remarks signal a significant shift in Ottawa's posture toward Washington as trade tensions continue to simmer.

Carney Signals Fundamental Shift in Canada's Economic Strategy

Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney delivered a pointed message to Canadians on Tuesday, declaring that the country's overwhelming economic dependence on the United States is a structural vulnerability that his government intends to dismantle. In a video address broadcast nationally, Carney framed the relationship not as a partnership of mutual strength, but as a liability that leaves Canada exposed to forces beyond its control.

"We have to take care of ourselves because we can't rely on one foreign partner," Carney said. "We can't control the disruption coming from our neighbors."

The remarks represent one of the most direct acknowledgments by a sitting Canadian prime minister that the historically close economic relationship with Washington carries inherent risks — risks that, in Carney's view, have become impossible to ignore amid renewed trade tensions and an increasingly unpredictable American political environment.

The Scale of Canada's US Economic Exposure

To understand the weight of Carney's warning, one need only examine the numbers. The United States absorbs roughly 75 percent of all Canadian exports, making it by far the dominant destination for Canadian goods. The two countries share what has long been described as the world's largest bilateral trading relationship, with hundreds of billions of dollars exchanging hands annually across the border.

Key Canadian industries — including automotive manufacturing, energy, agriculture, and lumber — are deeply intertwined with American supply chains, consumer markets, and regulatory frameworks. For decades, Canadian governments of both major parties treated this proximity as an unambiguous advantage, leveraging geographic and cultural closeness to build a prosperous export-driven economy.

But Carney, a former governor of both the Bank of Canada and the Bank of England and one of the world's most recognized economic policymakers, argues that this concentration of dependency has become a critical systemic risk.

What Changed — and When

The shift in Ottawa's thinking did not emerge overnight. A series of episodes over the past several years exposed the fragility of Canada's reliance on American goodwill. Repeated threats of broad tariffs on Canadian goods, disputes over softwood lumber, dairy access, and steel and aluminum, and the broader rhetorical hostility directed at Canada from Washington have collectively forced Canadian policymakers to reassess long-held assumptions.

Carney, who came to office promising a more assertive and economically self-sufficient Canada, has now made diversification a centerpiece of his government's agenda. His April 21 address is widely seen as a formal declaration of that policy direction, signaling to both domestic audiences and international partners that Canada is actively seeking to rebalance its economic relationships.

The Diversification Agenda: What Canada Is Pursuing

While Carney did not outline an exhaustive policy blueprint in his video address, the broad contours of Canada's diversification strategy have been taking shape since he assumed office. The approach rests on several interconnected pillars:

  • Deepening trade ties with the European Union: Canada's existing trade agreement with the EU, CETA, provides a framework, but Canadian officials believe far more can be done to increase the volume and diversity of transatlantic trade flows.
  • Expanding engagement in the Indo-Pacific: Japan, South Korea, India, and Southeast Asian markets represent enormous growth opportunities that Canada has historically underexploited relative to its economic potential.
  • Strengthening domestic supply chains: Carney's government has signaled interest in building more resilient internal economic infrastructure, reducing the need to route trade and manufacturing through American intermediaries.
  • Energy diversification: Canada has long debated how to reduce American control over the pricing and routing of Canadian energy resources. New pipeline infrastructure and liquefied natural gas export capacity are central to this ambition.
  • Investment in Canadian innovation: Reducing economic dependency also means growing industries — particularly in technology, clean energy, and advanced manufacturing — that are less exposed to American tariff and trade policy.

Domestic Reaction: Support Mixed With Caution

Carney's remarks drew immediate responses from across Canada's political and business communities. Many observers praised the candor, noting that successive Canadian governments had acknowledged the risks of US dependence privately while declining to state it so bluntly in public.

"This is a conversation Canada has needed to have for a generation," said one Ottawa-based trade economist who has advised multiple federal governments. "The question is whether the political will exists to make the structural changes required, or whether this remains aspirational."

Business groups expressed a more nuanced reaction. While broadly supportive of diversification efforts, several industry associations cautioned that reducing US economic ties is far easier to declare than to execute. The integrated nature of North American supply chains — particularly in the automotive sector — means that decoupling carries real short-term costs that businesses and workers would absorb.

"Nobody is arguing Canada should walk away from the American market," said a spokesperson for a major Canadian manufacturing lobby group. "But building alternative options is smart policy, and frankly, it's long overdue."

Opposition Response

Conservative critics were more skeptical, with several arguing that Carney's framing risks unnecessarily antagonizing Washington at a delicate moment. They contended that Canada's interests are better served by repairing and strengthening the bilateral relationship rather than signaling a desire to reduce it.

"The answer to a difficult neighbor is not to pretend you can simply find new ones overnight," said one Conservative parliamentarian. "These are complex, deeply embedded economic relationships that can't be unwound by a video address."

The Broader Geopolitical Context

Carney's statement arrives amid a broader global reassessment of economic interdependence. Across Europe, Asia, and the Americas, governments are grappling with how to build economic resilience without sacrificing the efficiencies of open trade. The COVID-19 pandemic, the war in Ukraine, and ongoing US-China tensions have all accelerated this rethinking.

In this context, Canada's push for diversification places it alongside a growing number of US allies who are quietly — and sometimes not so quietly — hedging against American unpredictability. The shift does not represent an anti-American posture so much as a pragmatic recognition that in an era of geopolitical volatility, concentration of dependency in any single partner carries unacceptable risks.

Carney himself has been careful to frame the initiative in precisely these terms, emphasizing that Canada remains a close friend and ally of the United States while insisting that friendship cannot substitute for strategic economic autonomy.

A Long Road Ahead

Analysts broadly agree that whatever the ambition, reorienting Canada's economic relationships will take years, if not decades, of sustained effort. Trade agreements must be negotiated, infrastructure must be built, and businesses must be incentivized to develop new market relationships that currently do not exist at scale.

The political challenge is equally formidable. Future governments may not share Carney's sense of urgency, and the gravitational pull of the American market — in terms of proximity, shared language, and cultural familiarity — will continue to make the US the path of least resistance for Canadian exporters.

Nevertheless, Carney's willingness to name the problem openly, and to commit his government to addressing it, marks a meaningful moment in the evolution of Canadian economic policy. Whether Tuesday's address proves to be the beginning of a genuine strategic transformation or a rhetorical statement that fades amid the pressures of governing remains to be seen.

What is clear is that the prime minister has placed Canada's vulnerability squarely in the center of the national conversation — and signaled that, under his leadership, the country intends to do something about it.
