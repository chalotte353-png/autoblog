Meet the 19-Metre Octopus That May Have Ruled Ancient Seas

New fossil evidence points to the existence of truly colossal octopuses that may have stretched up to 19 metres in length, prowling the ancient oceans more than 100 million years ago. Scientists believe these giants could have been among the most dominant predators of the Cretaceous seas. The discovery is reshaping our understanding of how complex and diverse marine life was long before modern creatures evolved.
James Liu
Space Correspondent
· April 24, 2026 · 5 min read

A Giant From the Deep Past

Imagine an octopus the length of a city bus, or roughly five times the size of the largest octopus alive today. Now imagine several of them ruling the oceans of prehistoric Earth. According to a compelling body of fossil evidence emerging from research teams around the world, this may not be as far-fetched as it sounds. Scientists are increasingly convinced that enormous octopuses, potentially stretching up to 19 metres from arm tip to arm tip, once prowled the seas during the Cretaceous period, more than 100 million years ago.

The findings, which have sent ripples of excitement through the palaeontology and marine biology communities, suggest that the evolutionary history of cephalopods, the group that includes octopuses, squids, and cuttlefish, is far more dramatic than previously imagined. While modern giant squids and colossal squids are known to reach impressive sizes, the idea that octopuses themselves once achieved such extraordinary dimensions opens an entirely new chapter in the story of life on Earth.

What the Fossil Record Reveals

Octopuses are notoriously difficult to find in the fossil record. Unlike creatures with hard shells or bones, octopuses are almost entirely soft-bodied, meaning they decompose rapidly after death and leave very little behind. This has historically made it extraordinarily challenging for scientists to piece together the evolutionary timeline of these intelligent invertebrates.

However, researchers have been examining a combination of fossilised remains, including rare impressions of soft tissue preserved in fine-grained sedimentary rock, along with indirect evidence such as fossilised bite marks and predation patterns on other ancient marine animals. Some sites in North America, Europe, and parts of Asia have yielded sediment layers containing what appear to be the preserved outlines of cephalopod bodies of extraordinary scale.

Additionally, fossilised remains of ancient fish and marine reptiles bearing distinctive circular wound patterns consistent with large sucker-bearing arms have added to the growing picture. These bite and grip marks, when analysed against known predation behaviours of modern octopuses, suggest an attacker of truly massive proportions.

The Science of Estimating Size

Reconstructing the size of an ancient creature from fragmentary evidence is always a challenge, but scientists use a range of established methodologies to arrive at credible estimates. By comparing fossilised tissue impressions with the known proportions of modern octopus species, researchers apply scaling models to extrapolate overall body size.

In the case of the proposed 19-metre giant, scientists have examined partial arm impressions that, when scaled against the arm-to-mantle ratios seen in living octopus species, suggest a mantle, or main body, of considerable size. The arm span of 19 metres would make this creature larger than a school bus and capable of exerting enormous force across a wide area of water.

The sheer scale of what the fossil evidence implies is staggering. If these estimates hold up to further scrutiny, we are looking at one of the largest predatory invertebrates ever to have lived on this planet.

While some researchers urge caution and note that estimates derived from soft-tissue impressions carry inherent uncertainty, the convergence of multiple lines of evidence from different fossil sites has given many in the scientific community cause for serious consideration.

Life in the Cretaceous Ocean

The Cretaceous period, which lasted from approximately 145 to 66 million years ago, was a time of extraordinary biological richness in the world's oceans. Sea levels were higher, temperatures were warmer, and vast shallow inland seas covered much of what is now dry land. These warm, nutrient-rich waters supported an abundance of life, from towering marine reptiles like mosasaurs and plesiosaurs to enormous sharks and vast schools of fish.

In such an environment, a giant octopus would have had access to an almost unlimited food supply. Modern octopuses are highly intelligent and adaptable hunters, capable of taking prey much larger than themselves using stealth, camouflage, and powerful arms. Scaled up to 19 metres, such an animal could potentially have targeted very large fish, other cephalopods, or even juvenile marine reptiles.

Intelligence and Behaviour at Scale

One of the most fascinating aspects of this discovery is what it implies about intelligence in the ancient oceans. Modern octopuses are considered among the most cognitively sophisticated invertebrates alive today, capable of problem-solving, using tools, and even demonstrating what some researchers describe as play behaviour. If truly giant octopuses existed 100 million years ago, the question arises as to whether they possessed comparable levels of intelligence relative to their size.

Brain-to-body ratios in cephalopods are among the highest seen in invertebrates, and some scientists speculate that evolutionary pressures toward large body size may have been accompanied by increasing neural complexity. Whether ancient giant octopuses were intelligent hunters or more passive ambush predators remains an open question, but one that is generating significant academic debate.

Why Octopuses Grew So Large

The phenomenon of gigantism in ancient marine animals is well documented. Many prehistoric ocean creatures achieved sizes far beyond those of their modern relatives, a trend scientists attribute to several factors:

  • Higher oxygen levels in ancient ocean environments, which may have supported the metabolic demands of larger bodies
  • Abundant food resources in warm, productive Cretaceous seas
  • Reduced competition in ecological niches not yet occupied by modern predator groups
  • Evolutionary pressure to outcompete other large predators through sheer size and strength
  • Longer lifespans potentially allowing individuals to grow to greater sizes before reaching reproductive maturity

These conditions created an oceanic world where growing large was a viable and advantageous strategy for survival. The giant octopus, if confirmed by further research, would join a remarkable gallery of prehistoric mega-fauna that includes the enormous shark Otodus megalodon and the vast marine reptiles of the Mesozoic era.

What This Means for Modern Science

Beyond the immediate fascination of imagining such a creature, the potential discovery of giant Cretaceous octopuses carries significant implications for several fields of science. For evolutionary biologists, it raises important questions about the trajectory of cephalopod evolution and whether the lineage leading to modern octopuses passed through a phase of extreme gigantism before reducing in size over millions of years.

For marine ecologists, it offers a reminder that the oceans of today represent only one possible configuration of marine life, and that the history of Earth's seas contains surprises that continue to challenge established models. Understanding what ecosystems once looked like, including who the top predators were, helps scientists model how changes in climate and ocean conditions drive evolutionary change.

For the broader public, the discovery is a powerful reminder of how much remains unknown about our planet's deep past. Despite centuries of scientific inquiry, the fossil record continues to yield extraordinary revelations, and the oceans, both past and present, remain among the most mysterious environments on Earth.

Ongoing Research and Future Discoveries

Research into ancient giant cephalopods is ongoing, with several international teams actively searching for additional fossil sites that may yield more complete remains. Advances in imaging technology, including high-resolution micro-computed tomography scanning, are allowing scientists to extract more information from fragmentary fossils than ever before. Meanwhile, chemical analysis of fossilised tissue impressions is providing new data about the physiology and diet of ancient cephalopods.

Scientists are particularly hopeful that new sites in regions with well-preserved Cretaceous marine sediments, including parts of the western interior of North America and certain locations in Europe, may yield more complete evidence in the coming years. Each new discovery adds another piece to a puzzle that, when complete, promises to reveal an ancient ocean world as dramatic and awe-inspiring as anything found in the modern deep sea.

A New Perspective on Ocean Prehistory

The story of the 19-metre octopus is ultimately a story about the limits of human knowledge and the extraordinary capacity of science to push those limits back. For most of recorded history, the ancient oceans have been imagined as populated by familiar archetypes, giant sea reptiles, enormous sharks, and vast schools of fish. The possibility that giant, intelligent, soft-bodied cephalopods were also players in this ancient drama adds a layer of complexity and wonder to our picture of prehistoric life.

As researchers continue to investigate the evidence and refine their understanding of what these creatures looked like, how they lived, and how they fit into the ecosystems of their time, one thing is already clear: the ancient seas were stranger, richer, and more spectacular than we ever imagined. And somewhere beneath the sediment of the ocean floor, more secrets are waiting to be found.
