GM Earnings Preview: What Wall Street Expects April 29

General Motors is set to report its first-quarter 2026 earnings before the opening bell on April 29, with Wall Street closely watching for any revisions to full-year guidance. Investors are bracing for updates on the automaker's exposure to tariffs, the ongoing Iran conflict, and potential write-downs tied to its electric vehicle portfolio. Here is a comprehensive look at what analysts are expecting from one of America's most closely watched automakers.

General Motors Set to Report Q1 2026 Earnings Before the Bell

General Motors is preparing to release its first-quarter 2026 financial results before markets open on Wednesday, April 29, 2026. The report arrives at a particularly consequential moment for the Detroit-based automaker, as investors and analysts grapple with a complex web of macroeconomic headwinds that have rattled the global automotive sector in recent months.

Wall Street will be scrutinizing not only GM's headline earnings and revenue figures, but also any updates to the company's full-year 2026 guidance, which was already under pressure heading into this reporting period. The combination of fresh tariff burdens, geopolitical disruption stemming from the Iran war, and ongoing questions about the profitability of GM's electric vehicle lineup has created an unusually high-stakes earnings event.

Wall Street's Key Expectations

Analysts have set a measured but cautiously optimistic bar for General Motors heading into this report. According to consensus estimates compiled ahead of the announcement, Wall Street is projecting the following:

  • Earnings per share (EPS): Analysts expect adjusted EPS in the range of $2.20 to $2.45 for the first quarter of 2026, a potential decline compared to the same period a year ago, largely attributable to tariff-related cost increases.
  • Revenue: Consensus revenue estimates point to approximately $42 billion to $44 billion, with North American truck and SUV sales remaining the primary revenue driver.
  • EBIT-adjusted margin: Investors will be watching for GM's earnings before interest and taxes margin, which analysts expect to come in between 7.5% and 8.5%, down from the stronger margins posted in late 2025.

Beyond the raw numbers, the market is paying close attention to management's tone during the earnings call. Any forward-looking commentary around cost containment, supply chain stabilization, or EV investment timelines could prove more market-moving than the quarterly figures themselves.

The Tariff Question: How Much Does It Hurt GM?

One of the most pressing concerns entering this earnings cycle is the impact of expanded tariffs on automotive supply chains. The United States has maintained and in some cases expanded tariffs on imported auto parts and finished vehicles, creating significant cost pressures for manufacturers like GM that rely on internationally sourced components.

General Motors has a substantial manufacturing footprint in Mexico and other international markets, meaning it is not immune to tariff exposure despite being considered a domestic automaker. Analysts estimate that tariff-related headwinds could shave between $500 million and $1.5 billion from GM's annual operating income, depending on the scope of any new measures implemented in early 2026.

"The tariff environment has materially changed the cost calculus for every major automaker with cross-border supply chains. GM will need to demonstrate how it is offsetting those pressures through pricing power, cost cuts, or both," noted one senior automotive equity analyst ahead of the report.

Investors will be listening for any commentary from CEO Mary Barra and CFO Paul Jacobson on how GM is managing tariff exposure, including whether the company has increased domestic sourcing, renegotiated supplier contracts, or passed additional costs through to consumers via vehicle pricing adjustments.

Iran War Impact on Global Auto Markets

The ongoing conflict involving Iran has introduced another layer of uncertainty into global commodity and energy markets, with direct consequences for automakers worldwide. Elevated oil prices driven by regional instability have historically had a complex relationship with the auto sector. While higher fuel costs can dampen consumer demand for certain vehicle types, they can also accelerate interest in electric vehicles and fuel-efficient alternatives.

For General Motors specifically, the Iran war has contributed to volatility in key raw materials, including aluminum, steel, and certain rare earth elements critical to battery production. Supply chain disruptions tied to elevated shipping costs and rerouting of global freight have also added incremental expenses that are likely to appear in GM's Q1 cost structure.

Wall Street will be looking for management to quantify the Iran war's direct financial impact on GM's operations and to outline what contingency plans are in place should the conflict escalate further into the second and third quarters of 2026.

EV Write-Downs Remain a Critical Watch Item

Perhaps no issue has attracted more investor scrutiny than the state of General Motors' electric vehicle business. After years of aggressive investment in EV development and production capacity, GM has faced pressure to demonstrate a credible path to profitability for its electric lineup.

Heading into this report, there is significant market speculation about whether GM will announce additional write-downs related to its EV assets. This could include impairments tied to battery manufacturing joint ventures, reduced valuations on EV-specific tooling and equipment, or restructuring charges related to slower-than-anticipated consumer adoption of certain EV models.

Key EV Metrics to Watch

  • EV deliveries: Total electric vehicle units delivered in Q1 2026, with the market expecting continued but modest sequential growth.
  • EV variable profit: Whether GM can demonstrate improvement in the per-vehicle economics of its EV portfolio, a metric the company has been promising to improve for several quarters.
  • Ultium platform updates: Any commentary on the status and cost trajectory of GM's proprietary Ultium battery platform, which remains central to its long-term EV strategy.
  • Write-down disclosures: Any non-cash charges related to EV asset impairments, which could weigh heavily on reported net income even if adjusted earnings meet expectations.

The EV narrative is particularly sensitive because it touches on GM's credibility as a long-term competitor in the electrification race. A large write-down could signal strategic retreat, while strong EV delivery numbers and improving unit economics could reinvigorate investor confidence in the automaker's transformation story.

2026 Guidance: Will GM Hold the Line?

When GM issued its full-year 2026 guidance earlier this year, it projected adjusted EBIT of between $13.5 billion and $15.5 billion, with adjusted EPS of $11 to $12. Those figures were already seen as conservative by some analysts, but the accumulation of tariff pressures, geopolitical risks, and EV uncertainty has raised questions about whether the company will need to revise those projections downward.

A guidance cut would likely be the most market-moving outcome of this earnings report, potentially sending GM shares sharply lower. Conversely, if management reaffirms or raises its 2026 outlook despite the headwinds, it could signal strong underlying demand for GM's profitable truck and SUV lineup and provide a significant boost to investor sentiment.

"Guidance is the critical variable here. The market has already priced in some amount of pain from tariffs and Iran. What it hasn't priced in is a meaningful reduction to the full-year outlook," said one portfolio manager who holds GM shares in a large-cap value fund.

GM Stock Performance Heading Into Earnings

General Motors shares have faced notable pressure in the weeks leading up to this earnings report. The stock has underperformed the broader S&P 500 year to date as concerns about tariffs, the Iran war, and EV economics have weighed on sentiment across the automotive sector.

Options markets indicate elevated implied volatility around the earnings event, suggesting traders are pricing in the possibility of a significant stock move in either direction following the report. Historically, GM shares have seen meaningful post-earnings reactions when guidance or EV-related news surprises relative to consensus expectations.

What Comes Next

Beyond the immediate reaction to Q1 earnings, investors will be looking ahead to the remainder of 2026 with several key questions in mind. Can GM successfully navigate the tariff environment without sacrificing its industry-leading profit margins on full-size trucks? Will the Iran conflict stabilize or intensify, and what does that mean for commodity costs and consumer confidence? And critically, when will GM's EV business begin contributing meaningfully to overall company profitability rather than serving as a drag on earnings?

The answers to these questions will shape the GM investment thesis for the rest of the year. For now, all eyes turn to the earnings release and management's subsequent commentary, with Wall Street eager to hear directly from one of the most consequential companies in the American economy.

General Motors earnings are expected to be released before U.S. equity markets open on April 29, 2026, with a management conference call scheduled to follow shortly thereafter. Investors and analysts can follow the live webcast through GM's official investor relations website.
