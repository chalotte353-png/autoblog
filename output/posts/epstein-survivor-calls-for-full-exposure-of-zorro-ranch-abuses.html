Epstein Survivor Calls for Full Exposure of Zorro Ranch Abuses

Rachel Benavidez, one of at least ten women who allege they were groomed or assaulted at Jeffrey Epstein's sprawling New Mexico compound, is speaking out with renewed urgency. She says that despite years of delays and legal obstacles, the full truth about what occurred at Zorro Ranch has yet to be told. Benavidez and other survivors are calling on investigators, lawmakers, and the public to keep the pressure on authorities to deliver accountability.
Patricia Morgan
Policy Correspondent
· April 21, 2026 · 5 min read

A Survivor's Determination to Be Heard

Nearly seven years after the death of convicted sex offender Jeffrey Epstein, one of his alleged victims is making clear that the passage of time has done nothing to diminish her resolve. Rachel Benavidez, who says she was groomed and assaulted at Epstein's Zorro Ranch compound in New Mexico, spoke publicly this week to reaffirm that the full story of what happened at the remote property has not yet been told and that it is not too late to expose it.

Benavidez is among at least ten girls and young women who have alleged they were subjected to abuse at the gated New Mexico estate, a sprawling property that Epstein used as one of several locations where prosecutors and civil attorneys say he ran a coordinated network of sexual exploitation. While much of the public attention surrounding Epstein's crimes has historically focused on his private island in the U.S. Virgin Islands and his Manhattan townhouse, survivors like Benavidez are insisting that what happened in New Mexico deserves equal scrutiny.

"The ranch is not forgotten. What happened there was real, it was systematic, and the people who made it possible are still out there. I will not stop until there is real accountability."

What Happened at Zorro Ranch

Zorro Ranch is a roughly 10,000-acre property located in Stanley, New Mexico, southeast of Albuquerque. Epstein purchased the compound in the early 1990s and used it for decades as a gathering place for a wide network of powerful associates. The property included a main residence, guest quarters, an airstrip, and various outbuildings, all surrounded by the kind of geographic isolation that survivors say made it easier for abuse to occur without outside interference.

Multiple accusers have described being transported to the property under false pretenses, often believing they were traveling for modeling opportunities, educational programs, or other seemingly legitimate purposes. Once there, they allege they were manipulated, coerced, and assaulted by Epstein and others.

The Pattern of Grooming

Legal advocates and therapists who have worked with Epstein Survivors describe a highly deliberate pattern of grooming that preceded the alleged assaults. The process, they say, often began with financial incentives or promises of career advancement aimed at young women from economically vulnerable backgrounds.

  • Survivors were often recruited through intermediaries who presented Epstein as a philanthropist or mentor.
  • Trips to Zorro Ranch were frequently framed as educational retreats or networking opportunities.
  • Once isolated at the compound, victims describe being subjected to escalating manipulation before alleged assaults occurred.
  • Some survivors say they were photographed or filmed without their consent during their time at the property.

Benavidez has stated that she was a teenager when she was first brought into contact with Epstein's network and that the grooming process was sophisticated enough that she did not fully understand what was happening to her until years later.

Why New Mexico Remains Central to the Investigation

Despite the significant legal proceedings that have taken place since Epstein's 2019 arrest and subsequent death, survivors and their legal representatives argue that New Mexico has received insufficient investigative attention compared to other jurisdictions. The state opened a formal investigation into Epstein's activities at Zorro Ranch in 2019, but progress has been slow and public disclosures have been limited.

New Mexico Attorney General Raul Torrez has previously indicated that the office continues to pursue leads connected to the property. However, survivors and advocacy groups have expressed frustration with what they describe as a lack of urgency and transparency in the process.

Federal and State Jurisdiction Complications

One of the persistent challenges in the Zorro Ranch investigation has been navigating the overlapping jurisdictions of federal and state law enforcement. Because Epstein's trafficking network spanned multiple states and countries, evidence and witness testimony gathered in New Mexico must often be coordinated with ongoing proceedings elsewhere, including the federal prosecution of Ghislaine Maxwell, who was convicted in 2021 and is currently serving a 20-year prison sentence.

Legal experts note that Maxwell's conviction, while historic, did not resolve questions about the broader network of individuals who may have participated in or facilitated abuse at locations like Zorro Ranch. Civil litigation filed by survivors has continued to surface names and details that federal prosecutors have not publicly addressed.

The Broader Network of Accountability

Benavidez and other advocates are increasingly focused not just on what happened at Zorro Ranch, but on who else may bear responsibility for enabling the alleged crimes there. Survivors have long maintained that Epstein did not operate alone and that a circle of wealthy and influential individuals provided financial support, logistical assistance, or direct participation in the abuse.

The release of court documents in recent years, stemming from a civil lawsuit filed by one of Epstein's accusers, has named dozens of individuals with alleged connections to the trafficking network. While many of those named have denied any wrongdoing, the documents have renewed calls from survivors for a comprehensive accounting of all those involved.

"People keep asking why we are still talking about this. We are still talking about it because nobody has fully answered for what happened. Not just Epstein. Not just Maxwell. Everyone who walked through those gates and knew what was going on."

Advocacy Organizations Amplifying Survivor Voices

A growing number of survivor advocacy organizations have taken up the cause of the Zorro Ranch accusers, arguing that their stories have been overshadowed by the more sensational coverage of Epstein's other properties. These groups are pushing for dedicated legislative hearings, increased funding for investigative resources in New Mexico, and greater legal protections for survivors who come forward.

Organizations focused on human trafficking prevention have also pointed to the Zorro Ranch case as an example of how predatory networks can operate in plain sight within rural and semi-isolated communities, exploiting geographic distance and institutional indifference.

The Path Forward for Survivors

For Rachel Benavidez and the other women who say they were victimized at Zorro Ranch, the path forward is neither simple nor certain. Civil litigation remains ongoing for some survivors, and new legal avenues may yet open as documents continue to be unsealed and investigations continue to develop. Several survivors have also become public advocates, working with lawmakers and legal reformers to change statutes that they say allowed Epstein and his associates to evade accountability for so long.

Benavidez has indicated she is prepared to testify publicly if given the opportunity and has urged other survivors who have not yet come forward to consider doing so, noting that legal protections for trafficking victims have improved significantly in recent years.

What remains consistent across the accounts of multiple Zorro Ranch survivors is a shared insistence that the property and what allegedly occurred there must not be reduced to a footnote in the broader Epstein story. For these women, Zorro Ranch is not a distant memory. It is an open wound that will remain so until those responsible are brought fully to account.

A Call to the Public and Policymakers

As the sixth anniversary of Epstein's death approaches, survivors are renewing their calls to the public to remain engaged with these cases. They argue that public attention is one of the most powerful tools available to ensure that investigators, prosecutors, and lawmakers do not allow momentum to fade.

For Benavidez, the message is straightforward. Justice delayed is not justice denied, she says, as long as survivors refuse to be silenced. The gates of Zorro Ranch may be closed, but the questions surrounding what happened behind them remain very much open.
