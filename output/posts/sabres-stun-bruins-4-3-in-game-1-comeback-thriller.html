Sabres Stun Bruins 4-3 in Game 1 Comeback Thriller

The Buffalo Sabres engineered one of the most memorable comebacks of the 2026 NHL playoffs, defeating the Boston Bruins 4-3 in Game 1. Tage Thompson and Mattias Samuelsson were the architects of a dramatic late-game surge that left TD Garden stunned and gave Buffalo an early series advantage.

Sabres Rally from the Brink to Steal Game 1 in Boston

BOSTON — In a performance that will be talked about in Buffalo for years to come, the Sabres erased a multi-goal deficit in the final period to defeat the Boston Bruins 4-3 in Game 1 of their 2026 NHL First Round playoff series on Monday night at TD Garden. The victory marks Buffalo's first playoff win in over a decade and sends a thunderous message to the rest of the league: this Sabres team refuses to quit.

Tage Thompson, the cornerstone of Buffalo's offensive identity, was nothing short of extraordinary down the stretch, while defenseman Mattias Samuelsson delivered what may prove to be the most pivotal moment of the entire series. The comeback, which unfolded with breathtaking speed in the third period, left a sold-out TD Garden crowd in stunned silence as the Sabres celebrated on enemy ice.

First Two Periods: Bruins Build a Comfortable Cushion

For forty minutes, this looked like a very different story. The Bruins came out with the kind of physical, suffocating game that has defined their identity for the better part of two decades. Boston forwards dominated puck possession in the offensive zone, cycling relentlessly and wearing down Buffalo's defense with wave after wave of pressure.

The Bruins opened the scoring midway through the first period on a power play conversion, silencing any early Sabres momentum. Boston added two more goals in the second period — the first a sharp-angle wrister that beat Sabres goaltender Ukko-Pekka Luukkonen to the short side, the second a deflection in front that bounced awkwardly off a skate and past the Finnish netminder.

Heading into the third period trailing 3-1, the Sabres faced long odds. Their season — and the promise of a genuine playoff run — appeared to hang by a thread. But that is precisely when Thompson and his teammates rewrote the script.

Thompson Takes Over: A Star Rises in the Playoffs

Tage Thompson has spent the better part of three regular seasons establishing himself as one of the NHL's elite power forwards, but Monday night offered him a first true postseason stage — and he seized it with both hands.

Thompson cut the deficit to 3-2 at the 6:47 mark of the third period, driving hard to the net off a cross-ice feed from JJ Peterka and burying a backhand shot that Bruins goaltender Jeremy Swayman barely saw. The goal immediately shifted the energy in the building, and the Buffalo bench erupted with a ferocity that telegraphed exactly what was coming next.

"I've been waiting for a moment like this," Thompson said in his post-game media availability. "The guys believed we could do it. We've been in tough situations before. You trust your preparation and you just go."

Thompson's leadership on the ice was matched by his composure under pressure. He won critical puck battles along the boards throughout the third period, anchored Buffalo's power play with clinical precision, and consistently found open teammates in the Bruins' defensive zone. His final line for the evening — one goal, two assists, and a plus-two rating — does not fully capture how central he was to everything the Sabres accomplished.

Samuelsson's Moment: A Defenseman Defines a Season

If Thompson was the engine of the comeback, Mattias Samuelsson was its crowning moment. The 25-year-old defenseman, who has grown into one of the most reliable two-way blueliners in the Eastern Conference, tied the game at 3-3 with 4:12 remaining in regulation on a booming slap shot from the left point that deflected off a Bruins defender and found the back of the net.

The goal was the product of smart positioning and an unrelenting offensive push from the Sabres, who had begun to pin Boston in their own zone with the kind of sustained zone time that had eluded them for most of the game. When Samuelsson unloaded his shot, the puck seemed to take on a life of its own — redirecting perfectly past a screened Swayman who had no chance to adjust.

"Sammy has been one of our best players all year," Thompson said of his teammate. "He has that big shot, and when he lets it go, things happen."

Samuelsson's goal set the stage for overtime, but the Sabres would not need it for long. Just 3:22 into the extra period, Dylan Cozens finished off a two-on-one rush — created by a pinching Samuelsson who refused to leave the offensive zone — with a precise wrist shot to the blocker side that ended the game and completed one of the most improbable finishes in recent playoff memory.

Luukkonen Holds the Fort When It Matters Most

Ukko-Pekka Luukkonen, who surrendered three goals in the first forty minutes, was exceptional when the Sabres needed him most. He stopped all 11 shots he faced in the third period and overtime, including a breakaway denial of Boston's David Pastrnak midway through the third that kept Buffalo's comeback hopes alive.

"UPL was huge for us," Sabres head coach Lindy Ruff said. "Three goals is not what he wanted, but he battled. He made the saves that gave us a chance."

Bruins Perspective: A Costly Lapse

For the Bruins, the loss stings precisely because it felt so preventable. Boston dominated possession statistics and outshot Buffalo 38-29 overall, yet a combination of defensive lapses, untimely penalties, and cold-blooded execution from the Sabres proved fatal.

Bruins head coach Jim Montgomery acknowledged the failure to protect the lead but remained measured in his assessment heading into Game 2.

"We gave them life in the third," Montgomery said. "That's on us. We know what we need to fix. This series is far from over."

Boston's penalty kill, which entered the playoffs ranked among the league's best, surrendered a key power play goal to Thompson in the third period — one of two man-advantage opportunities Buffalo converted on the night. Special teams, which many predicted would favor the Bruins heavily, ended up being a decisive equalizer for the Sabres.

What This Means for the Series

The Sabres now carry significant momentum heading into Game 2, scheduled for Wednesday night at TD Garden. Stealing a game on the road is a critical step for any underdog playoff team, and Buffalo's ability to respond to adversity suggests a resiliency that coaches and analysts have credited Ruff with building over the past two seasons.

  • Game 1: Buffalo Sabres 4, Boston Bruins 3 (OT) — Buffalo leads series 1-0
  • Game 2: Wednesday, April 23 at TD Garden, Boston
  • Game 3: Saturday, April 26 at KeyBank Center, Buffalo
  • Game 4: Monday, April 28 at KeyBank Center, Buffalo

For a franchise that has endured years of rebuilding and heartbreak, Monday night represented something far greater than a single playoff victory. It was proof that the Sabres have arrived as genuine contenders, and that Tage Thompson — cool, dominant, and clutch when the lights shine brightest — may be ready to carry this team all the way.

Key Stats from Game 1

  • Tage Thompson: 1G, 2A, +2, 5 shots on goal
  • Mattias Samuelsson: 1G, 1A, +3, game-tying goal with 4:12 left in regulation
  • Ukko-Pekka Luukkonen: 35 saves on 38 shots
  • Dylan Cozens: 1G (OT winner), 1A
  • Power play: Buffalo 2-for-4; Boston 1-for-3

The horn has sounded in Boston, and for the first time in a very long time, it is the Buffalo Sabres who are smiling.
