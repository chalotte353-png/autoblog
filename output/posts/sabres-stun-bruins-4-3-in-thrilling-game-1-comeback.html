Sabres Stun Bruins 4-3 in Thrilling Game 1 Comeback

The Buffalo Sabres pulled off a dramatic late comeback to defeat the Boston Bruins 4-3 in Game 1 of their 2026 NHL playoff series. Tage Thompson and Mattias Samuelsson were the architects of one of the most electrifying finishes of the postseason so far, giving Buffalo a stunning series lead heading into Game 2.

Sabres Storm Back to Steal Game 1 From Bruins in Boston

The Buffalo Sabres arrived in Boston on Tuesday night as underdogs, carrying the weight of a franchise starved for playoff success. They left TD Garden with something far more valuable than respect. They left with a 4-3 victory and a 1-0 series lead, the product of a furious, breathtaking third-period comeback that will be remembered for years in Western New York.

Trailing 3-1 entering the final frame, the Sabres refused to fold. Powered by the dynamic play of center Tage Thompson and the unexpected heroics of defenseman Mattias Samuelsson, Buffalo outscored Boston 3-0 in the third period to steal a game that, by all accounts, appeared to be firmly in the Bruins' control.

A Bruins Team That Seemed in Command

For the better part of two and a half periods, the Boston Bruins looked every bit the part of a seasoned playoff team playing on home ice. Their structure was suffocating, their transitions sharp, and their goaltending steady. Boston converted on the power play twice in the second period to extend what had been a modest lead into what felt like a comfortable cushion.

The atmosphere inside TD Garden was electric, the crowd sensing a commanding Game 1 performance from their squad. Boston's top line was dictating pace, and the Sabres, for long stretches, appeared to be playing not to lose rather than playing to win. It seemed only a matter of time before Boston put the game completely out of reach.

Then the third period began, and everything changed.

Thompson Ignites the Comeback

Tage Thompson has spent the better part of the last four seasons establishing himself as one of the most dangerous forwards in the entire National Hockey League. On Tuesday night, he reminded everyone exactly why he belongs in that conversation.

Thompson opened the Sabres' third-period rally with a powerful drive through the left circle, snapping a wrister that found the top corner at the 4:12 mark. The goal immediately shifted the energy on the Buffalo bench, and the Sabres began pressing with an urgency they had not shown earlier in the contest.

"We never stopped believing. That is the character of this group. We knew if we got one, we could get another, and we just kept building." Thompson said following the victory.

Thompson was not done. He added an assist on the tying goal and was on the ice for the eventual game winner, making him the undeniable catalyst of the comeback. His compete level in the third period was simply a different gear, and the Bruins had no answer for his combination of size, speed, and skill down the stretch.

Samuelsson Delivers the Defining Moment

If Thompson was the engine of Buffalo's comeback, Mattias Samuelsson was the unexpected spark plug that lit the fire. The young defenseman, known primarily for his steady and responsible two-way play, stepped into the role of postseason hero in remarkable fashion.

Samuelsson tied the game at 3-3 with a booming slap shot from the point at 11:47 of the third period, a bomb that beat the Bruins goaltender cleanly through a screen set up by the Sabres' forwards crashing the net. The goal sent the handful of Buffalo fans who had made the trip to Boston into a frenzy and visibly rattled the home crowd.

Then, with just under four minutes remaining in regulation, Samuelsson came back down the right side and wired a shot that deflected past the Boston netminder for what would prove to be the game-winning goal. It was a performance that nobody outside of the Sabres locker room could have fully anticipated, and it underscored the depth and the danger this Buffalo team carries into a series many had already written off as a forgone conclusion.

What Made Samuelsson's Performance So Significant

  • He recorded two goals from the blue line, exploiting Boston's failure to clear traffic in front of their own net.
  • His physical presence in the defensive zone kept Boston's most dangerous forwards in check throughout the final period.
  • His composure under pressure demonstrated a maturity that bodes well for the remainder of this series.
  • His offensive contribution from the back end gives Buffalo's coaching staff more options when drawing up special teams and late-game strategies.

Buffalo's Goaltending Held Firm When It Mattered

While Thompson and Samuelsson rightly earned the headlines, the Sabres' goaltender deserves significant credit for keeping Buffalo in the game long enough for the comeback to unfold. After allowing three goals, including two on the power play, he settled in and turned away every Boston shot he faced in the third period, including a pair of high-danger chances that could have slammed the door shut on any Sabres hopes.

His save percentage through the final twenty minutes was perfect, and his calm demeanor in net appeared to transfer to his teammates in front of him. In playoff hockey, goaltending wins series, and Buffalo's starter showed on Tuesday night that he is capable of delivering when the stakes are at their highest.

Boston Left to Regroup After Letting One Slip Away

For the Bruins, the loss stings in the way only a blown third-period lead can. Boston outplayed Buffalo for most of the night. They were the better team through forty minutes by nearly every measurable standard. Yet they find themselves trailing 1-0 in a series they were expected to control.

The questions now facing Boston's coaching staff are pointed and urgent. The power play, which looked so productive in the second period, went cold at the worst possible moment. The defensive structure that was locked in early began to leak in the third, and the Bruins' top forwards went quiet when the Sabres began applying pressure. These are fixable problems, but they must be addressed quickly before this series gets away from them.

Key Statistics From Game 1

  • Shots on goal: Boston 34, Buffalo 27
  • Power play goals: Boston 2 for 4, Buffalo 1 for 3
  • Third-period goals: Buffalo 3, Boston 0
  • Tage Thompson: 1 goal, 2 assists, plus-2 rating
  • Mattias Samuelsson: 2 goals, game-winning goal, plus-2 rating

Looking Ahead to Game 2

Game 2 of this first-round series is scheduled for Thursday night in Boston, with the Bruins desperate to even things up before the series shifts to KeyBank Center in Buffalo. The Sabres, meanwhile, will look to carry the momentum of their Game 1 triumph and further silence a home crowd that spent most of Tuesday evening expecting a very different outcome.

Buffalo's locker room, by all accounts, was a picture of controlled excitement following the final buzzer. These are players who have waited a long time for this stage, and the belief radiating from their group suggests they are not simply happy to be here. They came to win, and Game 1 proved, beyond any reasonable doubt, that they are fully capable of doing exactly that.

The 2026 Stanley Cup Playoffs have delivered countless memorable moments already, but few will rival what unfolded in Boston on Tuesday night. The Sabres are alive, dangerous, and for the first time in a very long time, playing their best hockey when it matters most.
