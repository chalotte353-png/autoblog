Markets are now closely watching what comes next for the central bank and its independence.

DOJ Officially Closes Criminal Investigation Into Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell

The Department of Justice has formally closed its criminal investigation into Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell, according to sources familiar with the matter, in a significant development that could have far-reaching consequences for U.S. monetary policy and the future leadership of the nation's central bank. The probe, which had quietly loomed over the Fed for months, was a primary reason why President Donald Trump's push to nominate former Fed Governor Kevin Warsh as Powell's replacement had stalled in Washington.

The closure of the investigation removes what had been described by insiders as a major procedural and political roadblock. With the inquiry now resolved, administration officials and Senate Republicans are expected to revisit the timeline for a formal Warsh nomination, though no official announcement has been made as of press time on April 25, 2026.

What the Investigation Entailed

Details surrounding the nature of the DOJ's criminal probe into Powell have remained closely guarded, with neither the Justice Department nor the Federal Reserve offering extensive public comment during the investigation's duration. Sources close to the matter indicated the inquiry was related to questions about financial disclosures and trading activity, echoing broader scrutiny that had engulfed the Fed in previous years when several regional bank presidents resigned following revelations about their personal investment decisions during sensitive policy periods.

Powell, who was first appointed to lead the Federal Reserve by President Trump in 2018 and later reappointed by President Biden in 2022, has consistently maintained that he operated fully within legal and ethical boundaries. His legal team had cooperated with investigators throughout the process, according to people briefed on the matter.

"The resolution of this matter reflects what we have maintained all along: that Chair Powell conducted himself with full integrity and in strict accordance with applicable law and ethics rules," a source close to Powell's legal representation said Saturday.

Kevin Warsh Nomination Back on the Table

The most immediate political consequence of the DOJ's decision is the renewed viability of Kevin Warsh as a potential successor to Powell. Warsh, 56, served on the Federal Reserve Board of Governors from 2006 to 2011 and has long been considered one of the most prominent figures in conservative monetary policy circles. President Trump made no secret of his preference for Warsh, viewing the former governor as someone more aligned with his administration's desire for lower interest rates and a more accommodative monetary stance.

However, the criminal probe created a complicated political dynamic. Senate Republicans were reluctant to move forward on a Warsh confirmation process while an open DOJ investigation into the sitting Fed Chair remained unresolved, fearing that the optics of replacing someone under federal scrutiny could backfire or invite accusations of political interference in law enforcement. With that concern now removed, sources say Warsh's path to confirmation has become considerably clearer.

Financial markets have been on edge for weeks amid a combination of geopolitical and economic pressures. Investors have been monitoring developments closely, as uncertainty over Fed leadership compounds broader anxieties. Earlier this month, Dow Futures Plunge 350 Points as Iran War Tensions Surge, illustrating just how sensitive equity markets have become to any additional layer of uncertainty, whether geopolitical or institutional.

Powell's Term and the Question of Continuity

Jerome Powell's current term as Federal Reserve Chair runs through May 2026, meaning the question of succession is not merely hypothetical. It is an immediate operational reality that the White House, the Senate, and financial markets must all grapple with simultaneously.

Supporters of Powell argue that replacing him at a moment of ongoing inflation management and global financial instability would be deeply disruptive. Critics within the Trump administration, by contrast, have argued that the Fed has been too slow to cut rates and that new leadership aligned with a growth-oriented policy framework is overdue.

The Federal Open Market Committee's rate decisions in recent months have already drawn criticism from the White House, with administration officials privately and publicly pressing for more aggressive rate reductions. Warsh is seen as someone who would bring a different approach, though analysts caution that any new Fed chair still operates within the constraints of the central bank's dual mandate and institutional independence.

Market Reaction and Broader Economic Context

The news of the DOJ's decision broke late Friday, with initial market reactions playing out in overnight futures trading. Analysts say the removal of uncertainty around Powell's legal status is broadly viewed as a stabilizing factor in the short term, even as questions about the ultimate direction of monetary policy remain unresolved.

Energy markets, already under significant pressure from geopolitical developments in the Middle East, are another area where investors are keeping a close watch. As reported earlier this week, Oil Prices Jump After Strait of Hormuz Setbacks, adding another layer of inflationary concern to an already complex economic picture that the Federal Reserve will need to navigate regardless of who sits at its helm.

Economists note that leadership transitions at the Fed, even well-managed ones, tend to introduce periods of uncertainty that can affect everything from bond yields to mortgage rates. The sooner the administration moves to formalize a nomination, the sooner markets can begin to assess what policy continuity or change might look like.

Senate Dynamics and the Path Forward

Senate confirmation of a new Fed chair is not guaranteed to be smooth. While Republicans hold a majority, several moderate members of the caucus have expressed concern about the perception of politicizing the Federal Reserve. Warsh himself has worked in recent months to present himself as an institutionalist who would defend central bank independence, a message calibrated specifically to address those concerns.

Democratic senators are widely expected to oppose the nomination, framing it as an attempt by the Trump administration to install a loyalist at the most powerful financial institution in the world. The confirmation hearings, whenever they occur, are likely to be among the most closely watched economic policy events of the year.

Meanwhile, the country continues to process a series of sobering national stories that have dominated headlines. The tragic Louisiana Father Kills 8 Children in Shreveport Mass Shooting sent shockwaves across the nation this week, underscoring the weight of the many simultaneous crises confronting American institutions and policymakers at every level of government.

What Comes Next

With Powell's term expiring in weeks and the DOJ investigation now closed, the administration faces a narrow window of time to act. White House officials are said to be preparing briefing materials for a formal Warsh nomination, though the exact timing remains fluid. The Senate Banking Committee would need to hold hearings and advance the nomination before a full floor vote.

In the interim, Powell is expected to continue fulfilling his duties as Fed chair. The Federal Reserve has a scheduled policy meeting in May, and Powell is expected to preside over it regardless of what announcements may come from the White House in the days ahead.

The dismissal of the Justice Department's criminal probe into Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell marks a significant development in the ongoing debate over central bank independence, removing a major source of uncertainty that had weighed on financial markets and monetary policy deliberations in recent weeks.

As April draws to a close, the resolution of the Powell investigation stands as one of the most consequential developments in American economic governance in recent memory. What happens next, in the halls of the Senate and at the Federal Reserve itself, will set the tone for U.S. monetary policy for years to come.
