'Man on Fire' Netflix Review: Yahya Abdul-Mateen II Shines

Netflix's ambitious seven-hour adaptation of 'Man on Fire' arrives with Yahya Abdul-Mateen II taking on the role made iconic by Denzel Washington, delivering a raw and emotionally charged performance that commands attention. Director Kyle Killen stretches the revenge thriller into a sprawling limited series format, giving the story room to breathe in ways the original film never could. The result is a morally complex, visually stunning piece of prestige television that cements Abdul-Mateen II as one of the most compelling dramatic actors working today.
Olivia Brown
Film Critic
· May 01, 2026 · 6 min read

A Familiar Story Reimagined for the Streaming Era

When Netflix announced it was adapting A.J. Quinnell's 1980 novel into a limited series, skepticism was understandable. The property had already been filmed twice, most memorably in Tony Scott's 2004 film starring Denzel Washington, a visceral, kinetic revenge thriller that became a cultural touchstone. The question was never whether the story deserved another telling, but rather whether any actor could carry the emotional weight of a role so thoroughly associated with one of Hollywood's greatest performers. As of May 1, 2026, that question has been definitively answered.

Yahya Abdul-Mateen II does not attempt to replicate Denzel Washington's performance. He does something far more interesting. He dismantles the mythology of John Creasy and rebuilds it from the bones outward, crafting a portrait of a broken man that is quieter, more introspective, and ultimately more devastating than anything the films managed to achieve. In a streaming landscape crowded with prestige adaptations chasing awards glory, Man on Fire stands apart as something genuinely felt.

Seven Hours to Tell One Man's Story

Creator and showrunner Kyle Killen, whose previous work has demonstrated a consistent interest in psychological complexity and moral ambiguity, makes excellent use of the extended runtime. Spread across seven episodes ranging from forty-five minutes to nearly seventy, the series takes its time establishing John Creasy as a fully realized human being before the catalytic violence that sets the plot in motion.

The first two episodes are almost entirely devoted to character work. We watch Creasy arrive in Mexico City, haunted by an unnamed war and years of mercenary work that have left him spiritually hollow. He is drinking too much, sleeping too little, and moving through the world like a man who has quietly accepted that his story is nearly over. Abdul-Mateen II plays these early scenes with extraordinary restraint, conveying a lifetime of buried trauma through small gestures and silences rather than big dramatic declarations.

When he accepts a position as a bodyguard for the Ramos family and begins forming a bond with young Lupita, played with remarkable depth by twelve-year-old newcomer Isabella Vega, the series finds its emotional heartbeat. Their relationship develops organically over several episodes, and when the kidnapping finally occurs at the end of episode three, it lands with the full weight of everything the series has carefully constructed.

The Revenge Mechanics

Episodes four through seven shift tone considerably, embracing the propulsive action thriller elements that fans of the source material will be expecting. Killen and his team of directors, which includes Uta Briesewitz and Millicent Shelton, demonstrate genuine fluency with large-scale action sequences. The violence is brutal and purposeful, never gratuitous but always meaningful in context.

What distinguishes the series from a standard revenge narrative is its willingness to question the morality of Creasy's rampage. Unlike the 2004 film, which largely celebrated its protagonist's vengeance as righteous catharsis, the Netflix adaptation consistently holds the narrative at a slight moral distance. We root for Creasy, but Killen never lets us fully off the hook for doing so.

The series asks uncomfortable questions about what justice actually means when it is administered by a man with blood already on his hands, and it does so without offering easy answers.

This thematic complexity is supported by a strong ensemble that surrounds Abdul-Mateen II's performance. Demian Bichir is quietly menacing as the corrupt official whose network Creasy must dismantle, while Zoe Saldana brings unexpected dimension to a role that could easily have been a simple love interest. Their scenes together carry a melancholy charge that the series earns rather than assumes.

Production Values That Match the Ambition

Netflix has invested significantly in the production, and it shows on screen. The Mexico City locations are captured with stunning specificity by cinematographer Natasha Braier, whose work transforms the city into a character in its own right. The color palette shifts deliberately across the series, moving from the warm amber tones of Creasy's early days with the Ramos family to the cold, desaturated blues that dominate the revenge episodes.

The production design is equally impressive, creating a world that feels lived-in and authentic without tipping into the kind of poverty tourism that lesser productions sometimes fall into when depicting Latin American settings. The series treats its Mexican characters and setting with genuine respect, grounding the international thriller framework in specific cultural reality.

The score by Arca is a genuine surprise, blending electronic textures with traditional Mexican instrumentation to create something that feels both contemporary and deeply rooted in place. It is among the most distinctive television scores of the year so far.

Where the Series Stumbles

Not everything works. The sixth episode, which pauses the narrative momentum for an extended flashback sequence explaining Creasy's wartime history, feels like a structural miscalculation. The information it conveys is valuable, but the timing disrupts the tension that the series has carefully built, and the episode struggles to justify its runtime.

There are also moments in the final episode where the series reaches for emotional resolution that it has not quite earned. Certain character arcs are tied up with a neatness that sits uneasily alongside the moral complexity that has defined the preceding hours. These are relatively minor complaints in the context of a seven-hour work, but they prevent the series from achieving the full greatness that its best moments seem to promise.

  • Standout episode: Episode five, which contains the series' most devastating set piece and Abdul-Mateen II's finest scene
  • Most improved element from source material: The depth and specificity of the supporting characters
  • Biggest risk that pays off: The extended slow-burn of the first two episodes
  • Element that does not quite land: The flashback structure of episode six

Yahya Abdul-Mateen II: A Star-Making Turn

It is impossible to discuss this series without returning, repeatedly, to Abdul-Mateen II's central performance. The actor, who first gained widespread attention in Watchmen and has built an impressive body of work across film and television, delivers what may be the finest performance of his career to date.

What makes the performance remarkable is its physical and emotional specificity. Abdul-Mateen II has clearly done extensive preparation, and his physicality changes convincingly across the series as Creasy transforms from a broken, alcohol-softened man into something harder and more dangerous. But it is the interior work that distinguishes the performance. His eyes carry the full history of the character in every scene, conveying grief, purpose, self-loathing, and occasional flickers of something almost like hope.

The scenes between Abdul-Mateen II and Isabella Vega are the emotional core of the series, and both performers rise to the occasion with work that feels completely genuine. Their dynamic avoids the sentimentality that plagued the 2004 film, finding something more complicated and more true in the relationship between a damaged man and a child who sees past his damage.

Awards conversations for the upcoming cycle should begin and end here. This is a leading performance that deserves to be discussed alongside the finest dramatic television work of the decade.

Final Verdict

Man on Fire is not a perfect limited series, but it is an ambitious, emotionally resonant, and frequently brilliant one. Kyle Killen has found in this material a vehicle for exploring ideas about violence, redemption, and moral complicity that the original film only touched on, and he has assembled the talent to do those ideas justice.

For viewers willing to invest the time, the series rewards patience with a viewing experience that lingers long after the final episode concludes. For streaming television in 2026, that is a considerable achievement, and one that suggests Netflix's bet on this adaptation was very well placed indeed.

You can stream Man on Fire on Netflix beginning May 1, 2026, with all seven episodes available simultaneously.

Rating: 4 out of 5 stars
