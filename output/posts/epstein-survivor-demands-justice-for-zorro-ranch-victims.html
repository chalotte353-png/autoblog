Epstein Survivor Demands Justice for Zorro Ranch Victims

Rachel Benavidez, one of at least ten women who allege they were groomed or assaulted at Jeffrey Epstein's secluded New Mexico compound, is speaking out with renewed urgency. She insists that the full truth about what occurred at Zorro Ranch has yet to be told and that accountability remains within reach. Her voice is joining a growing chorus of survivors demanding that investigators, lawmakers, and the public refuse to let the case fade into silence.

Policy Correspondent
· April 21, 2026 · 5 min read

A Survivor Refuses to Stay Silent

More than six years after the death of convicted sex offender Jeffrey Epstein, one of his alleged victims is stepping forward with a powerful message: the story of what happened at his sprawling New Mexico property is far from over. Rachel Benavidez, who says she was among the young women groomed and assaulted at Epstein's gated compound known as Zorro Ranch, is calling on authorities, the public, and fellow survivors to keep pushing for the full truth to be revealed.

Benavidez is among at least ten girls and young women who have alleged they were targeted at the remote ranch located in the high desert outside of Stanley, New Mexico. While Epstein himself died in a federal detention facility in August 2019 before he could stand trial on federal sex trafficking charges, survivors like Benavidez argue that his death did not close the chapter on accountability for those who participated in or enabled his alleged criminal enterprise.

What Happened at Zorro Ranch

Zorro Ranch, a roughly 10,000-acre compound that Epstein acquired in the early 1990s, became one of several locations tied to his alleged abuse network. Unlike his more frequently discussed residences in New York City, Palm Beach, and his private Caribbean island, the New Mexico property received comparatively less public and prosecutorial attention in the years following Epstein's arrest and death.

Survivors and their attorneys have long argued that Zorro Ranch was a deliberate choice by Epstein precisely because of its remoteness. Situated far from urban centers and public scrutiny, the property allegedly served as a location where young women could be brought under the guise of employment or educational opportunities, only to find themselves in exploitative and deeply traumatic situations.

Benavidez has described being brought to the ranch as a teenager, saying she was made to feel that what was happening to her was normal or expected. She has spoken about the psychological manipulation involved, including how adults in positions of trust were used to facilitate access to Epstein and his associates.

The Isolation Factor

Legal advocates working with Zorro Ranch survivors have emphasized that the isolation of the property was not incidental but strategic. Key factors that made the location especially troubling include:

  • Geographic remoteness: The ranch's location made it difficult for victims to seek help or leave without assistance from their abusers.
  • Limited local oversight: The sparse population and limited law enforcement presence in the surrounding area reduced the likelihood of outside intervention.
  • Controlled environment: Witnesses have described a tightly managed compound with staff who were allegedly complicit in maintaining secrecy.
  • Psychological conditioning: Survivors have reported being groomed over extended periods before being brought to the property, making resistance more difficult.

Calls for Renewed Investigation

Despite the significant legal proceedings that have already occurred, including the federal conviction of Ghislaine Maxwell in December 2021 on charges including sex trafficking of minors, survivors and their advocates argue that justice remains incomplete. Maxwell, Epstein's former girlfriend and associate, was sentenced to 20 years in federal prison, but survivors note that her prosecution focused primarily on activities in other locations.

Benavidez and others are now pushing for investigators to take a harder look at the New Mexico compound specifically. They argue that the full scope of who visited the ranch, what occurred there, and who may have been aware of or participated in the alleged abuse has never been properly examined in a courtroom or public forum.

"What happened in New Mexico did not happen in a vacuum. There were people who knew. There were people who participated. And some of those people have never been held responsible for what they did or allowed to happen."

That sentiment, widely shared among the survivor community, has fueled ongoing legal efforts to compel the release of documents, communications, and visitor logs associated with Zorro Ranch. Several civil lawsuits referencing the property remain active, and attorneys representing multiple victims have argued in court filings that evidence related to the ranch has been inadequately preserved or disclosed.

The Legal Landscape in 2026

The legal fight over Epstein-related accountability has continued to evolve years after his death. Civil litigation has resulted in settlements with Epstein's estate for a number of victims, but many survivors have expressed dissatisfaction with the process, saying it prioritized financial resolution over public disclosure of what actually happened.

Benavidez has been vocal about her own experience navigating the civil settlement process, describing it as retraumatizing and insufficiently transparent. She has called for independent oversight of remaining evidence and for congressional action to ensure that any documents or records related to Epstein's properties, including Zorro Ranch, are made fully available to the public.

The Broader Movement for Accountability

Benavidez's renewed public campaign comes amid a broader moment of reflection on how institutions, law enforcement, and powerful individuals enabled Epstein to operate for decades with apparent impunity. Critics have pointed to multiple earlier opportunities to stop his alleged abuse, including a controversial 2008 plea deal in Florida that shielded Epstein and potential co-conspirators from federal prosecution.

The survivors who have spoken publicly over the years represent only a fraction of those who may have been victimized. Advocates estimate that the full number of women and girls affected by Epstein's alleged network could be significantly higher than what has been officially documented, in part because many victims have chosen not to come forward publicly due to fear, stigma, or concerns about retraumatization.

Organizations supporting survivors of trafficking and sexual exploitation have pointed to the Epstein case as a critical example of how wealth and social connections can be weaponized to delay and obstruct justice. They argue that Benavidez's continued advocacy is not only personally meaningful but serves a broader public interest in understanding how such networks operate and how they can be dismantled.

What Survivors Want Next

Those who have spoken publicly about Zorro Ranch have outlined specific demands as they continue their fight for accountability:

  • Full disclosure of visitor records: Survivors and their attorneys want comprehensive logs of who visited the New Mexico property during Epstein's ownership released to the public.
  • Independent investigation: Several advocates have called for a special investigative body separate from prosecutors who may have conflicts of interest tied to earlier Epstein cases.
  • Legislative protections: Survivors are pushing for extended statutes of limitations in states where abuse occurred, ensuring that any individuals not yet charged have not escaped accountability simply due to the passage of time.
  • Acknowledgment and apology: Beyond legal remedies, many survivors have expressed a desire for formal public acknowledgment from institutions that failed to act when warning signs were present.

A Voice That Will Not Be Silenced

For Benavidez, speaking out is an act of both personal healing and public responsibility. She has described the decision to go public as difficult but necessary, driven by the conviction that silence only benefits those who perpetrated or enabled harm. She has also expressed a desire to support other survivors who may still be struggling to come forward, saying that her own experience taught her how powerful community and belief can be in the recovery process.

Her message to other potential witnesses or survivors who may have information about Zorro Ranch or Epstein's broader network is straightforward: it is not too late. Investigations can still be reopened. Evidence can still be uncovered. And the people who have not yet been held accountable may still face consequences if the public and its institutions refuse to move on prematurely.

As the Epstein case enters yet another chapter, Rachel Benavidez stands as a reminder that the survivors at the center of this story are not historical footnotes. They are living people whose calls for justice deserve to be heard, taken seriously, and acted upon with the full force of the law and the moral weight of a society that claims to protect its most vulnerable members.
