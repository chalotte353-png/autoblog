US Stablecoins Threaten Emerging Markets, Central Bankers Warn

Central bankers from emerging economies have issued stark warnings about the rapid expansion of US dollar-pegged stablecoins, arguing that the growth of these crypto assets under the Trump administration threatens monetary sovereignty and risks enabling widespread criminal misuse. The concerns come as Washington accelerates its push to cement dollar dominance through digital assets, leaving developing nations increasingly vulnerable to economic disruption they have little power to control.

Global Central Bankers Sound Alarm Over Dollar-Pegged Crypto Assets

Central bankers across emerging economies are raising urgent alarms about the accelerating spread of US dollar-pegged stablecoins, warning that the Donald Trump administration's aggressive push to expand American crypto dominance poses serious risks to monetary sovereignty, financial stability, and law enforcement efforts in developing nations worldwide.

Senior officials from central banks spanning Latin America, sub-Saharan Africa, and Southeast Asia have voiced growing concern that the explosion of stablecoin adoption, supercharged by a permissive regulatory environment in Washington, could fundamentally undermine their ability to conduct independent monetary policy, manage inflation, and control capital flows within their borders.

The warnings, coordinated through multilateral financial institutions and expressed at international banking forums this week, represent the most forceful collective pushback yet from the developing world against what many policymakers describe as a new and potentially more dangerous form of financial dollarisation.

The Stablecoin Surge Under Trump

Since the Trump administration took office in January 2025, the regulatory landscape for stablecoins in the United States has shifted dramatically. Legislation moving through Congress has prioritised enabling major technology companies and financial institutions to issue dollar-backed digital tokens with minimal friction, while the administration has openly positioned stablecoins as a tool for extending dollar hegemony in the global financial system.

Total stablecoin supply has grown to exceed $400 billion as of early 2026, with the vast majority pegged to the US dollar. Platforms facilitating stablecoin transactions have expanded aggressively into emerging markets, where populations dealing with currency instability and high inflation have proven eager adopters of dollar-denominated digital assets.

In countries such as Argentina, Nigeria, Turkey, and Vietnam, stablecoin usage has grown at rates far outpacing adoption in developed economies. For ordinary citizens in these nations, the appeal is clear: dollar-pegged tokens offer a relatively simple means of preserving savings against domestic currency depreciation and hyperinflation.

However, central bankers argue that what may appear as a financial lifeline for individual savers is, at a systemic level, a serious threat to the economic tools governments rely upon to manage national economies.

The Dollarisation Dilemma

Dollarisation, the process by which a foreign currency, typically the US dollar, displaces the domestic currency in a country's economic transactions and savings, has long been a concern for policymakers in unstable economies. Historically, it has occurred gradually through informal channels. Central bankers now warn that stablecoins risk accelerating this process at unprecedented speed and scale.

When large segments of a population hold and transact in dollar-pegged stablecoins rather than the local currency, central banks lose critical levers of monetary control. Interest rate decisions become less effective, foreign reserve management grows more complicated, and governments face reduced ability to respond to economic shocks through currency adjustments.

According to a senior official from Brazil's central bank, speaking at a closed-door session of an international monetary conference in Zurich this week, the current trajectory of stablecoin growth represents a qualitative shift in the dollarisation threat.

"We are no longer talking about informal dollar cash economies. We are talking about seamlessly accessible, smartphone-enabled dollar systems that can reach every citizen with an internet connection. The speed and scale are categorically different from anything we have faced before," the official said.

Criminal Misuse and Regulatory Gaps

Beyond the macroeconomic risks, central bankers and financial regulators from emerging economies have expressed deep concern about the criminal exploitation of stablecoins. Law enforcement agencies in multiple developing nations have reported a significant uptick in the use of dollar-pegged crypto assets for money laundering, sanctions evasion, drug trafficking proceeds, and corruption-related capital flight.

The decentralised or semi-decentralised nature of many stablecoin platforms, combined with jurisdictional gaps between US regulators and foreign authorities, creates what officials describe as an extremely difficult enforcement environment. Transactions that originate or terminate in emerging markets often pass through infrastructure that is legally anchored in the United States or offshore jurisdictions with limited cooperation agreements.

Key Criminal Risks Identified by Central Bankers

  • Money laundering: Illicit funds can be rapidly converted into stablecoins, moved across borders, and reconverted into local currency with limited traceability under current frameworks.
  • Sanctions evasion: State and non-state actors subject to international sanctions have been identified using dollar stablecoins to access the US-denominated financial system indirectly.
  • Tax evasion and capital flight: Wealthy individuals and corporations are increasingly using stablecoins to move capital out of emerging economies without triggering traditional reporting mechanisms.
  • Terrorist financing: Several national intelligence services have flagged stablecoin platforms as vectors for financing extremist networks operating across developing regions.

Diverging Interests: Washington vs. the Developing World

The tensions between Washington's stablecoin ambitions and the concerns of emerging market central banks reflect a broader geopolitical divide over the future of the international monetary system. The Trump administration has been explicit in its view that dollar-denominated stablecoins serve American strategic interests by deepening global reliance on dollar infrastructure and countering the influence of Chinese digital payment systems and potential alternatives to dollar hegemony.

For US policymakers and the domestic crypto industry, the proliferation of dollar stablecoins in emerging markets is largely presented as a win for American financial influence and a demonstration of the private sector's ability to extend dollar reach where traditional banking has failed.

Critics, however, argue that this framing ignores the real costs imposed on sovereign nations attempting to manage their own economic destinies. Several emerging market finance ministers have begun coordinating responses, with discussions ongoing about potential capital controls targeting stablecoin inflows, requirements for local licensing of stablecoin platforms, and bilateral agreements to improve information sharing on suspicious transactions.

Calls for International Regulatory Coordination

A growing coalition of central bankers is calling for urgent international regulatory coordination to address the systemic risks posed by dollar stablecoins. The Bank for International Settlements has been urged to convene an emergency working group, while the International Monetary Fund is expected to update its guidance on stablecoin risks in its forthcoming global financial stability report.

Proposals under discussion include:

  • Mandatory disclosure requirements for stablecoin issuers operating in jurisdictions beyond the United States
  • International standards for know-your-customer and anti-money laundering compliance for stablecoin platforms
  • Mechanisms allowing affected central banks to receive real-time transaction data from major stablecoin operators
  • A multilateral framework for freezing and recovering stablecoin assets linked to criminal activity across borders

Whether such proposals can gain traction in the current political environment remains deeply uncertain. The Trump administration has shown little appetite for multilateral financial governance frameworks that could constrain American crypto sector growth, and major stablecoin issuers have powerful lobbying presences in Washington that have successfully shaped domestic legislation to date.

What Comes Next

For many emerging market economies, the stablecoin challenge is no longer a theoretical future risk but an urgent present reality. Central banks in several countries are accelerating their own central bank digital currency programmes in part as a defensive measure, hoping that a credible domestic digital currency option can reduce the appeal of dollar stablecoins for ordinary citizens.

However, experts caution that central bank digital currencies alone are unlikely to reverse the trend, particularly in countries with persistent inflation problems and currency instability that make the dollar's appeal structural rather than temporary.

As stablecoin adoption continues its rapid expansion and the regulatory environment in Washington remains favourable to growth, the warnings from emerging market central bankers are likely to grow louder. The question of who bears the costs of America's digital dollar ambitions may become one of the defining financial policy debates of the coming decade.

The divide between stablecoin enthusiasm in Washington and alarm in emerging market capitals is widening, and without meaningful international dialogue, the consequences for the global financial system could prove both profound and difficult to reverse.
