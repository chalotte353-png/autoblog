US Stablecoins Threaten Emerging Markets, Central Bankers Warn

Central bankers from emerging economies have issued stark warnings about the rapid expansion of US dollar-pegged stablecoins, arguing that the growth of these crypto assets under the Trump administration threatens monetary sovereignty and risks enabling widespread criminal misuse. The concerns come as Washington accelerates its push to cement dollar dominance through digital assets, leaving developing nations increasingly vulnerable to economic disruption they have little power to control.

Central Bankers Sound Alarm Over Dollar-Pegged Crypto Assets

Senior central bank officials from across the developing world have raised urgent concerns about the accelerating proliferation of US dollar-pegged stablecoins, warning that the Trump Administration's aggressive promotion of crypto assets poses a serious and potentially irreversible threat to monetary stability in emerging market economies.

The warnings, delivered through a series of coordinated statements and international financial forums this week, reflect deepening anxiety among policymakers in Africa, Latin America, Southeast Asia and Eastern Europe about the pace at which dollar-denominated stablecoins are penetrating local financial systems, often bypassing regulatory oversight entirely.

At the heart of the concern is a phenomenon economists call dollarisation, where citizens and businesses in a given country begin conducting transactions and storing savings in a foreign currency, in this case the US dollar, rather than their domestic one. Stablecoins, which are crypto tokens pegged one-to-one with the dollar, have made this process faster, cheaper and more accessible than at any previous point in history.

The Trump Effect on Global Crypto Expansion

Since returning to the White House in January 2025, President Donald Trump has moved aggressively to position the United States as the global capital of cryptocurrency. His administration has rolled back key regulatory restrictions, granted broad licences to stablecoin issuers and publicly endorsed several major crypto projects. The president himself has financial ties to digital asset ventures, a fact that critics argue represents a significant conflict of interest in shaping national policy.

The practical result has been a dramatic expansion in the issuance and global reach of stablecoins. Total stablecoin market capitalisation has surpassed one trillion dollars in 2026, with dollar-pegged tokens accounting for the overwhelming majority of that figure. Tether and USD Coin remain dominant, but a new wave of institutionally backed entrants has entered the market following Washington's regulatory green light.

For Emerging Markets, this expansion is not abstract. In countries experiencing high inflation, currency volatility or limited access to traditional banking services, stablecoins have become an attractive alternative for ordinary citizens seeking to protect their purchasing power. That behaviour, while understandable at an individual level, creates systemic risks that central bankers say they are ill-equipped to manage.

The Dollarisation Dilemma

Dollarisation has historically been a slow, difficult process requiring physical dollar bills, formal banking relationships or complex foreign exchange transactions. Stablecoins have fundamentally changed that equation. With a smartphone and a crypto wallet, a citizen in Lagos, Buenos Aires or Nairobi can now hold and transact in digital dollars instantaneously, without ever interacting with a local bank or currency exchange.

Central bankers warn that the implications for monetary policy are severe. When a significant portion of a country's economic activity migrates to a foreign currency, the central bank loses much of its ability to manage inflation, set interest rates effectively or respond to economic shocks. Countries that have experienced full or partial dollarisation in the past, such as Ecuador and Zimbabwe, have found it extraordinarily difficult to reverse the process.

A senior official at one major emerging market central bank, speaking on condition of anonymity, described the situation as a slow-moving crisis that policymakers are struggling to get ahead of. The official noted that stablecoin adoption among the unbanked and underbanked populations in their country had grown by more than 300 percent in the past eighteen months alone.

Capital Flight and Currency Pressure

Beyond the long-term structural risks, central bankers also point to more immediate threats. As citizens convert local currency savings into stablecoins, demand for domestic currency falls, putting downward pressure on exchange rates and potentially triggering inflationary spirals. In economies already dealing with external debt burdens denominated in dollars, further currency depreciation can rapidly become a debt crisis.

The concern is particularly acute for smaller economies with limited foreign exchange reserves. If stablecoin adoption accelerates rapidly, these countries may find themselves facing a dual crisis of currency collapse and depleted reserves, with few tools available to respond.

Criminal Misuse and Regulatory Blind Spots

Beyond the macroeconomic risks, central bankers and law enforcement officials have highlighted the growing use of stablecoins to facilitate criminal activity in Emerging Markets. The same features that make stablecoins attractive to ordinary users, including speed, low cost, pseudonymity and cross-border functionality, also make them powerful tools for money laundering, sanctions evasion, ransomware payments and illicit capital flows.

Investigative bodies across several developing regions have reported significant increases in stablecoin-linked financial crime over the past two years. In some cases, criminal networks have used stablecoins to move large sums across borders with minimal detection, exploiting gaps between national regulatory regimes and the decentralised nature of blockchain technology.

The problem is compounded by the fact that many emerging market jurisdictions lack the technical capacity, legal frameworks and international cooperation mechanisms needed to monitor and investigate stablecoin transactions effectively. While major economies like the European Union and the United Kingdom have introduced comprehensive crypto asset regulation, much of the developing world remains in a regulatory grey zone.

US Regulatory Stance Draws Criticism

Critics argue that Washington bears a measure of responsibility for the global spillover effects of its domestic crypto policies. By actively promoting dollar-pegged stablecoins and weakening oversight requirements for issuers, the Trump Administration has, in effect, exported financial instability to economies far less equipped to absorb the consequences.

  • Reduced anti-money laundering requirements for certain stablecoin issuers have been identified as a key vulnerability
  • Weakened know-your-customer rules have made it easier for bad actors to access dollar-denominated crypto assets
  • Lack of international coordination on stablecoin standards has left developing countries exposed without recourse
  • Lobbying influence from major crypto firms in Washington has shaped policy in ways that prioritise US industry interests over global financial stability

Several emerging market finance ministers have formally requested that the International Monetary Fund and the Bank for International Settlements take a stronger stance on the cross-border risks of stablecoin proliferation. Early indications suggest both institutions are preparing updated guidance, though the timeline for any binding international framework remains unclear.

Calls for Coordinated International Action

In response to these mounting pressures, a coalition of central banks from emerging and developing economies has called for urgent multilateral action. Their demands include mandatory disclosure requirements for stablecoin issuers operating in their jurisdictions, greater information sharing between financial intelligence units, and the establishment of a global registry of stablecoin transactions to improve transparency.

Some officials have gone further, calling for the right to block or restrict access to specific stablecoin platforms that fail to comply with local regulatory standards. Such measures would be technically challenging to implement but are increasingly being discussed as a necessary tool for preserving monetary sovereignty.

There is also growing interest among emerging market central banks in accelerating the development of their own central bank digital currencies as a domestic alternative to dollar stablecoins. Several countries have launched pilot programmes, though none have yet achieved the scale or convenience that commercial stablecoins currently offer.

A Defining Moment for Global Finance

The debate over stablecoins and their impact on Emerging Markets reflects a broader tension at the heart of the digital finance revolution. Technologies that offer genuine benefits, including financial inclusion, reduced transaction costs and greater economic participation, can simultaneously undermine the stability and sovereignty of the very communities they claim to serve.

For the central bankers raising the alarm, the urgency is real. The window to establish meaningful guardrails is narrowing as adoption accelerates and the political will in Washington to impose restrictions on a booming domestic industry shows little sign of materialising.

What is clear is that the consequences of inaction will be felt most severely not in the boardrooms of Silicon Valley or on Wall Street, but in the daily economic lives of hundreds of millions of people in the world's most vulnerable economies. Whether the international community moves quickly enough to address those consequences remains one of the most pressing and consequential questions in global finance today.
