HEVC Licensing Fees Explained: Why Vendors Drop Support

HEVC promised to revolutionize video compression, but its tangled web of patent pools and unpredictable royalty demands has frustrated device makers for over a decade. Understanding why vendors keep abandoning H.265 support requires a clear-eyed look at how licensing actually works in practice. The situation in 2026 remains as complicated as ever, with no clean resolution in sight.

The Promise and the Problem with HEVC

When the High Efficiency Video Coding standard, widely known as HEVC or H.265, was finalized in 2013, it represented a genuine technical achievement. The codec promised roughly double the compression efficiency of its predecessor, H.264, meaning the same video quality at approximately half the file size. For streaming services, broadcasters, and device manufacturers, that kind of efficiency gain was enormously appealing.

More than a decade later, however, HEVC adoption remains fragmented and contentious. Major vendors have periodically stripped HEVC support from their products, open-source communities have largely pivoted to royalty-free alternatives, and the licensing landscape continues to confuse even seasoned technology executives. To understand why, it is essential to examine how HEVC licensing actually functions and why the fee structures involved create such persistent friction.

How HEVC Patent Licensing Is Structured

Unlike some technology standards governed by a single licensing body, HEVC is subject to claims from multiple competing patent pools as well as numerous individual patent holders who operate entirely outside of any pool structure. This fragmented ownership model is the root cause of most of the confusion and frustration surrounding the codec.

As of 2026, there are three primary patent pools asserting rights over HEVC implementations. Each pool independently collects royalties, and a vendor wishing to fully license HEVC is generally expected to negotiate with all of them separately. This is not a hypothetical concern. Companies have faced royalty demands from all three pools simultaneously, meaning the true cost of HEVC support is not a single, transparent fee but rather the cumulative sum of multiple overlapping obligations.

The Three Major Patent Pools

  • HEVC Advance: This pool was formed in 2015 and initially caused significant controversy when it announced royalty rates that were dramatically higher than what the industry had anticipated. HEVC Advance has since revised its rates downward in response to market pressure, but it remains one of the more aggressive licensing entities in the space.
  • MPEG LA: The same organization that administered H.264 licensing established a separate pool for HEVC. MPEG LA's HEVC pool is generally considered more predictable, but it does not cover all essential patents, which means licensing through MPEG LA alone does not provide comprehensive protection.
  • Velos Media: Formed from patents originally held by several major technology companies, Velos Media represents a third pool that vendors must account for in their licensing calculations. Velos Media has been notably active in pursuing licensees.

Beyond these three pools, there are additional patent holders who have chosen not to join any pool and instead pursue licensing independently. This means that even a vendor that successfully negotiates agreements with all three major pools may still face legal exposure from outlier patent holders asserting HEVC-essential claims.

What Royalties Actually Cost in Practice

The precise royalty rates vary by pool and by the nature of the product being sold, but industry observers have noted some general patterns that help explain vendor frustration. For consumer devices such as televisions, smartphones, and set-top boxes, royalties are typically assessed on a per-unit basis. Rates from individual pools have ranged historically from fractions of a cent to several dollars per unit, depending on the pool and the product category.

When those per-unit fees are stacked across three pools, the cumulative cost can become meaningful, particularly for high-volume, low-margin hardware manufacturers. A budget television maker selling tens of millions of units annually at thin profit margins faces a very different calculus than a premium brand that can absorb licensing costs more easily.

The problem is not that any single royalty demand is necessarily unreasonable in isolation. The problem is the unpredictability of stacking multiple pools on top of each other, plus the ever-present threat of demands from entities outside the pools entirely. Budget planning becomes nearly impossible under those conditions.

Software vendors face a somewhat different structure. Streaming applications, encoders, and media players may be subject to royalties based on usage volumes, subscription counts, or flat licensing fees depending on their deployment model. The lack of standardization across pools means that a software company must negotiate separately with each entity and may reach very different terms with each one.

Why Vendors Choose to Drop HEVC Support

Given this complexity, the business decision to remove or limit HEVC support often makes rational economic sense, even when it creates short-term product limitations. Several factors drive vendors toward this choice.

Legal Uncertainty and Litigation Risk

Even vendors that believe they have adequate licenses in place face the risk of litigation from patent holders who dispute the scope or validity of existing agreements. HEVC-related litigation has been ongoing in multiple jurisdictions, and the cost of defending against patent suits, regardless of their ultimate merit, is substantial. For smaller vendors, the litigation risk alone may be sufficient to justify avoiding the codec entirely.

The Royalty-Free Alternative Landscape

The emergence of AV1, developed by the Alliance for Open Media and made available without royalty obligations, has given vendors a compelling alternative path. AV1 achieves compression efficiency comparable to HEVC, and major streaming platforms including video services operated by the largest technology companies have adopted it aggressively. Hardware acceleration for AV1 is now widely available in consumer devices, removing one of the historical arguments in favor of HEVC.

Similarly, VVC, or Versatile Video Coding, represents the next-generation successor to HEVC and unfortunately appears to be inheriting many of the same patent pool fragmentation problems. This has pushed more of the industry toward royalty-free options as a matter of long-term strategic hygiene.

Platform-Specific Dynamics

Some vendors have found that their specific platform ecosystems provide workarounds that reduce the practical impact of dropping direct HEVC support. By relying on operating system-level codec support, vendors can in some cases shift the licensing responsibility to the OS provider rather than bearing it directly. This approach is imperfect and platform-dependent, but it has influenced how some companies have structured their HEVC strategies.

How HEVC Implementation Works in 2026

Despite the licensing complexity, HEVC is far from extinct. It remains widely used in broadcast television, physical media including Ultra HD Blu-ray, and certain streaming workflows. In many regions, HEVC-encoded content is the default for high-definition and 4K broadcasts, meaning that device manufacturers in those markets have no practical choice but to support it.

For these vendors, the licensing cost is treated as a cost of doing business, built into product pricing and absorbed across large unit volumes. Premium television manufacturers, set-top box makers serving cable and satellite operators, and camera manufacturers all continue to implement HEVC support as a baseline requirement for market participation in key segments.

What has changed significantly is the calculation for software developers and emerging hardware categories. New applications built primarily for internet-based video distribution are increasingly defaulting to AV1 or VP9, with HEVC included only when backward compatibility or specific platform requirements demand it. In these contexts, the licensing overhead is seen as a tax on legacy compatibility rather than a necessary investment in core functionality.

The Path Forward for the Industry

There is no obvious near-term resolution to the structural problems created by HEVC's fragmented patent landscape. The patent pools have shown limited appetite for consolidation, and individual patent holders outside the pools have every incentive to maintain their independent positions as long as HEVC remains commercially significant.

The most likely long-term outcome is a gradual shift in which HEVC maintains its position in legacy broadcast and physical media applications while newer internet-native content ecosystems migrate almost entirely to royalty-free alternatives. This transition is already well underway in 2026 and is expected to accelerate as AV1 hardware acceleration becomes universal across consumer device categories.

For developers and technology decision-makers evaluating codec strategies today, the practical advice from industry experts is consistent: understand which patent pools claim coverage for your specific use case, budget for negotiations with multiple entities, and factor in ongoing litigation risk as a real operational cost. Where royalty-free alternatives deliver adequate technical performance, the business case for choosing them over HEVC is increasingly difficult to argue against.

The HEVC story is ultimately a cautionary tale about what happens when a technically superior standard is released into a fragmented and adversarial intellectual property environment. The codec works exceptionally well. The business of licensing it has never worked cleanly, and a decade of market experience suggests it never will.
