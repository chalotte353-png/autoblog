Trump Reclassifies Medical Marijuana in Historic Policy Shift

In a landmark move that advocates have pursued for decades, the Trump administration has officially reclassified state-licensed medical marijuana as a less-dangerous substance under federal law. The order, signed by the acting attorney general, marks one of the most significant shifts in American drug policy in generations. The decision separates medical cannabis from Schedule I status, where it had been grouped alongside heroin since the early 1970s.

A Landmark Moment in American Drug Policy

WASHINGTON, April 24, 2026 — In a move that is reverberating across the legal, medical, and political landscape of the United States, President Donald Trump's acting attorney general has signed a formal order reclassifying state-licensed medical marijuana as a less-dangerous drug under federal law. The decision represents one of the most sweeping changes to American drug policy in more than fifty years, ending a classification that critics have long called scientifically indefensible and socially harmful.

The reclassification removes state-licensed medical cannabis from Schedule I of the Controlled Substances Act, the most restrictive category reserved for substances deemed to have no accepted medical use and a high potential for abuse. For decades, marijuana shared that designation with heroin, a classification that advocates, physicians, and researchers argued was deeply inconsistent with the growing body of scientific evidence supporting cannabis-based treatments.

The announcement was made Friday morning and immediately drew reactions from lawmakers, patient advocacy groups, Cannabis Industry executives, and civil liberties organizations across the political spectrum.

What the Reclassification Means in Practice

The order specifically applies to marijuana that is produced, distributed, and consumed within the framework of state-licensed medical programs. This distinction is critical, as it stops short of a blanket federal legalization of cannabis but acknowledges the legitimacy of the medical programs that thirty-eight states and the District of Columbia have established over the past three decades.

Under the new classification, state-licensed medical marijuana is expected to be moved to a Schedule III designation, which covers substances recognized as having accepted medical uses and a moderate to low potential for dependence. Other Schedule III substances include ketamine and certain anabolic steroids.

Key Implications of the Reclassification

  • Research Expansion: Scientists and universities will face significantly fewer federal barriers to conducting clinical research on cannabis and its therapeutic applications.
  • Banking Access: Medical marijuana businesses operating legally under state law may gain broader access to banking and financial services, addressing a longstanding challenge for the industry.
  • Tax Relief: Cannabis businesses could see relief from Section 280E of the Internal Revenue Code, a tax provision that has prevented state-licensed cannabis companies from deducting standard business expenses.
  • Criminal Justice: While not retroactive in scope, the change signals a federal acknowledgment that medical cannabis users and operators should not be treated as narcotics traffickers under the law.
  • Patient Access: Veterans and other patients who rely on federal healthcare programs may eventually see pathways opened for cannabis-based treatment options.

A Long Road to Reform

The push to reclassify marijuana did not begin with the Trump Administration. Advocacy efforts stretching back to the 1970s, combined with mounting scientific research and shifting public opinion, gradually built the pressure that ultimately produced Friday's order. The Obama administration declined to reschedule cannabis despite internal reviews. The Biden administration initiated a formal rescheduling review through the Drug Enforcement Administration, a process that laid much of the legal groundwork for the current action.

What makes Friday's announcement remarkable is the political context in which it occurred. The Trump Administration, known for its tough-on-crime rhetoric during its first term in office, has now delivered what may be the single most consequential federal cannabis policy action in American history. Political analysts noted that the move reflects both evolving Republican voter sentiment on marijuana and the administration's interest in regulatory reforms that benefit small business operators.

"This is a day that patients, doctors, and advocates have waited for since Richard Nixon signed the Controlled Substances Act into law. Treating medical marijuana the same as heroin was never based on science. Today, that injustice is finally corrected."

Reactions From Across the Political Spectrum

Responses to the announcement poured in swiftly from all corners of the political world. Supporters celebrated the move as long overdue, while critics raised concerns about federal oversight, youth access, and public health safeguards.

Progressive lawmakers praised the step but called it incomplete, arguing that full descheduling and the expungement of prior cannabis convictions remain necessary to achieve true justice. Several senators from both parties indicated they would push for follow-up legislation to codify the reclassification into statute and protect it from potential reversal by future administrations.

Social conservative groups expressed concern that the reclassification would open the door to broader legalization, warning that weakened federal signals could contribute to increased youth marijuana use. Some law enforcement organizations echoed those concerns, calling for strong federal guidance to accompany the regulatory change.

The Cannabis Industry responded with cautious optimism. Shares in publicly traded cannabis companies surged in early trading Friday, with several major multistate operators posting double-digit gains. Industry leaders emphasized that significant regulatory uncertainty still remains, particularly regarding interstate commerce and federal licensing frameworks.

The Science Behind the Decision

A formal review conducted by the Department of Health and Human Services concluded that cannabis does not meet the criteria for Schedule I classification. The review, submitted to the DEA prior to the current administration, found that marijuana has accepted medical uses in treatment in the United States and that its potential for abuse is more consistent with Schedule III substances than with the most dangerous drugs in the federal system.

Medical organizations representing physicians, oncologists, neurologists, and pain management specialists have long argued that cannabis-based treatments offer meaningful benefits for patients dealing with chronic pain, epilepsy, cancer-related nausea, multiple sclerosis, and a range of other conditions. The reclassification signals that federal drug policy is finally beginning to align with that clinical consensus.

Outstanding Legal and Regulatory Questions

Despite the historic nature of Friday's action, significant legal and regulatory questions remain unresolved. The reclassification applies to state-licensed medical marijuana, but the legal status of recreational cannabis remains unchanged at the federal level. Federal employees, military personnel, and individuals in federally regulated industries will continue to be subject to drug testing policies that treat any cannabis use as prohibited.

Additionally, the specific regulatory framework governing how Schedule III cannabis will be produced, labeled, distributed, and monitored under federal oversight has not yet been detailed. The DEA and FDA are expected to issue further guidance in the coming months, a process that legal experts predict could take well over a year to complete.

What Comes Next

The acting attorney general indicated in a statement accompanying the order that the administration views the reclassification as part of a broader effort to align federal regulations with scientific evidence and the realities of state law. Further regulatory action is anticipated, and the administration has indicated it will engage with Congress on companion legislation.

Advocates are already looking beyond reclassification, setting their sights on full federal descheduling, automatic expungement for past convictions, and the creation of a regulated interstate cannabis market. While those goals remain distant, Friday's order has fundamentally altered the trajectory of the conversation.

For the millions of Americans who use state-licensed medical cannabis to manage serious health conditions, and for the thousands of small business owners who have built legal enterprises under state law, April 24, 2026, marks the beginning of a new era in American drug policy. Whether that era delivers on its promise will depend on the regulatory choices made in the months and years ahead.
