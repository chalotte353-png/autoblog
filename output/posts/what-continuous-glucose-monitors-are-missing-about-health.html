What Continuous Glucose Monitors Are Missing About Health

Continuous glucose monitors have surged in popularity among people without diabetes, promising real-time insight into metabolic health. But researchers who study wearable health data say the constant stream of numbers may be doing more harm than good, leaving users anxious, confused, and no healthier than before.

Cybersecurity Analyst
· April 27, 2026 · 6 min read

The Promise and the Problem With Always-On Health Monitoring

Walk into any gym, wellness retreat, or biohacking community in 2026, and you are likely to spot small circular patches adhered to the backs of upper arms. These are continuous glucose monitors, or CGMs, devices originally designed to help people living with diabetes track their blood sugar levels around the clock. In recent years, however, they have crossed over into the mainstream wellness market, worn by athletes, executives, and health-conscious consumers who have never received a diabetes diagnosis in their lives.

The appeal is intuitive. More data should mean more knowledge, and more knowledge should mean better decisions. But researchers who specialize in wearable health technology are raising serious questions about whether that logic holds up in practice, particularly when the data in question is as complex and context-dependent as blood glucose readings.

As someone who has spent years studying how people interpret and act on wearable health data, I have seen a consistent pattern emerge: continuous monitoring does not automatically lead to better health behaviors. In many cases, it leads to the opposite.

Understanding What CGMs Actually Measure

A continuous glucose monitor works by inserting a tiny filament just beneath the skin that measures interstitial fluid glucose levels. It sends readings to a paired smartphone or display device every few minutes, producing a continuous graph of glucose fluctuations throughout the day and night.

For a person managing Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes, this information is genuinely life-saving. Knowing when blood sugar is spiking or crashing allows for timely interventions with insulin, food, or physical activity. The clinical value in that population is well-established and profound.

For a metabolically healthy person, the picture is far murkier. Glucose levels naturally fluctuate throughout the day in response to meals, stress, sleep, exercise, and even temperature changes. These fluctuations are normal physiological responses, not warning signs. Yet when a healthy person watches their glucose spike after eating a banana and then sees it return to baseline within an hour, the monitor's alert tone and rising graph line can trigger genuine alarm.

The Data Overload Dilemma

This is the core issue that my research, and the work of colleagues across multiple institutions, keeps surfacing. The human brain is not well-equipped to make meaningful decisions from a relentless stream of numerical data without clinical training and interpretive context. When we hand people a tool that delivers hundreds of data points per day about a bodily function they have never previously monitored, we are not automatically empowering them. We may be overwhelming them.

In studies examining how non-diabetic users respond to CGM data, participants frequently reported:

  • Significant anxiety following normal postmeal glucose spikes
  • Restrictive eating behaviors driven by fear of seeing numbers rise
  • Disrupted sleep from overnight alerts that turned out to be clinically insignificant
  • Confusion about what constitutes a healthy versus unhealthy reading
  • A tendency to attribute emotional states or energy levels to glucose readings even when no meaningful correlation existed

One participant in a study I led described checking her CGM app more than forty times in a single day, adjusting her meals and activities based on readings that her physician later confirmed were entirely within a healthy range. She lost weight, but she also lost her enjoyment of food and developed what her doctor characterized as disordered eating patterns.

What the Marketing Does Not Tell You

The companies selling CGMs to wellness consumers have built compelling narratives around concepts like metabolic flexibility, glucose stability, and personalized nutrition. Their marketing materials are sophisticated, peppered with scientific-sounding language and testimonials from athletes and influencers who credit the devices with transforming their energy, focus, and body composition.

What these materials rarely explain clearly is that the research supporting CGM use in metabolically healthy populations is thin. Most of the robust clinical evidence for continuous glucose monitoring involves people with diagnosed diabetes or prediabetes. Extrapolating those findings to the general wellness market requires a significant leap of faith, one that the current evidence base does not fully support.

"We are essentially running a massive uncontrolled experiment on healthy consumers," said one endocrinologist whose research focuses on metabolic monitoring technology. "The devices are safe to wear, but the behavioral and psychological consequences of the data they generate in non-clinical populations are not well understood."

The Signal-to-Noise Problem

Part of what makes CGM data so difficult to interpret without clinical guidance is the sheer amount of noise in the signal. Glucose readings can be temporarily elevated by stress hormones, a poor night of sleep, dehydration, or even the way a person is positioned when the sensor takes a reading. A reading that looks alarming on a graph may reflect nothing more than the cortisol spike that comes from waking up in the morning, a phenomenon so common it has a clinical name: the dawn effect.

Without understanding these nuances, users are left trying to make sense of data that requires years of medical training to interpret correctly. They turn to online forums, social media influencers, and wellness coaches, sources that may be enthusiastic but are rarely equipped to provide genuinely evidence-based guidance.

What Wearable Health Data Actually Needs to Be Useful

None of this means wearable health technology is without value. Properly designed and properly contextualized, these tools have real potential. The problem is not the technology itself. The problem is the gap between what the technology measures and what users believe it is telling them.

For wearable health data to genuinely improve outcomes, several things need to change:

  • Clinical integration: CGM data should ideally be reviewed with a healthcare provider who can help interpret readings in the context of an individual's overall health profile.
  • Smarter alert thresholds: Devices used by non-diabetic populations should apply different alert parameters than those designed for clinical use, avoiding unnecessary alarms for normal physiological variation.
  • Contextual education: Users should receive robust education about glucose physiology before they begin monitoring, so they understand what they are seeing before anxiety sets in.
  • Regulatory clarity: Clearer guidance from health authorities about appropriate use cases for CGMs outside of diabetes management would help both consumers and the companies selling these devices.

The Broader Lesson for the Wearable Technology Industry

The enthusiasm around continuous glucose monitors reflects a broader cultural moment in which we have come to believe that more data is always better, that self-quantification is inherently empowering, and that technology can substitute for the nuanced, individualized guidance that a knowledgeable clinician provides. These beliefs are understandable. They are also, in important ways, mistaken.

The wearable technology industry has produced genuinely useful innovations. Heart rate monitoring has helped people exercise more safely and detect arrhythmias. Sleep tracking has raised awareness about the importance of rest. Step counting has motivated sedentary people to move more. These are meaningful contributions to public health.

But there is a meaningful difference between a step counter and a device that monitors a complex metabolic biomarker every few minutes and delivers that data without adequate interpretive support. The former provides simple, actionable information. The latter delivers complexity that most users are not equipped to navigate alone.

As CGMs become cheaper and more widely available, and as other continuous biomarker monitors enter the consumer market, tracking everything from cortisol to ketones to inflammatory markers, the industry and the medical community need to have an honest conversation about the difference between data and insight. Monitoring a constant stream of numbers does not automatically help people make better decisions about their health. In the absence of context, education, and clinical support, it may simply generate a new source of confusion and needless anxiety.

That is not a reason to abandon wearable health technology. It is a reason to be far more thoughtful about how we design, market, and support it. The goal, after all, is not to produce more data. The goal is to help people live healthier lives. Those two things are not the same, and confusing them does a disservice to both technology and to the people who trust it with their health.
