Dances With Wolves Actor Jailed 37 Years for Sexual Assault

Nathan Chasing Horse, best known for his role in the 1990 film Dances With Wolves, has been sentenced to a minimum of 37 years in federal prison after being convicted of multiple counts of sexual assault. The 49-year-old exploited his position as a self-proclaimed spiritual leader to prey upon Indigenous women and girls across several US states. The case has drawn widespread attention to the ongoing crisis of violence against Indigenous communities in North America.

Former Actor Receives Lengthy Prison Term for Decades of Abuse

Nathan Chasing Horse, the former actor who gained international recognition for his supporting role in the acclaimed 1990 film Dances With Wolves, has been sentenced to a minimum of 37 years in federal prison following his conviction on multiple counts of sexual assault and sex trafficking. The 49-year-old appeared in a United States federal court on April 29, 2026, where a judge handed down the substantial sentence after a trial that exposed years of predatory behavior targeting vulnerable members of Indigenous communities.

Prosecutors argued that Chasing Horse used his celebrity status and his self-appointed role as a spiritual leader within Indigenous communities to establish a position of power and trust, which he then systematically abused. Victims and their advocates described the sentencing as a long-overdue moment of accountability for a man who had used cultural authority and spiritual manipulation as tools of exploitation for many years.

The Crimes: Exploitation of Trust and Spiritual Authority

According to testimony presented during the trial, Chasing Horse cultivated a persona as a healer and spiritual guide, drawing in followers from multiple Indigenous nations across the United States and Canada. He allegedly used ceremonial practices and spiritual traditions as a means of isolating and controlling his victims, many of whom were young women and girls who trusted him as a figure of cultural and spiritual significance.

Federal prosecutors outlined a disturbing pattern of behavior that spanned more than two decades. Chasing Horse is said to have coerced victims into sexual acts under the pretense of spiritual rituals, threatened those who considered speaking out, and used his influence within tight-knit communities to silence potential witnesses.

"He used sacred traditions as a weapon against the very people those traditions were meant to protect. The harm he caused goes beyond the individual victims and has wounded entire communities," a prosecutor stated during the sentencing hearing.

The charges against him included sex trafficking of minors, sexual assault, and obstruction of justice. Investigators from multiple jurisdictions collaborated over several years to build the case, gathering testimony from numerous survivors who had previously been afraid to come forward.

Background: From Hollywood to Spiritual Fraud

Chasing Horse rose to public prominence when he appeared alongside Kevin Costner in Dances With Wolves, the Oscar-winning epic that depicted life among the Lakota Sioux people in the nineteenth century. The film was celebrated for its respectful portrayal of Indigenous culture, and Chasing Horse, himself of Lakota descent, was seen at the time as a symbol of authentic representation.

In the years following his acting career, Chasing Horse reinvented himself as a spiritual leader and healer, traveling between Indigenous reservations and communities. He established what law enforcement would later describe as a cult-like following, with devoted supporters who believed in his claimed spiritual powers and his supposed ability to communicate with ancestors and spirits.

This carefully constructed persona gave him almost unchecked access to vulnerable individuals, including minors, across reservation lands in states including Nevada, Montana, and South Dakota. The jurisdictional complexities of cases involving crimes on tribal lands have historically made prosecutions more difficult, a factor that investigators acknowledged contributed to the length of time it took to bring Chasing Horse to justice.

The Broader Crisis of Violence Against Indigenous Women

The case has reignited urgent national conversations about the disproportionate levels of violence faced by Indigenous women and girls in the United States. Advocacy groups have long documented that Native American women experience rates of violence significantly higher than those of any other demographic group in the country, and that cases involving Indigenous victims often receive less attention from law enforcement and the media.

Organizations working on behalf of Indigenous communities have highlighted several systemic issues that allowed Chasing Horse to operate for so long without facing legal consequences:

  • Jurisdictional gaps: The overlapping and sometimes unclear jurisdiction between tribal, state, and federal authorities can delay or complicate the investigation and prosecution of crimes on tribal lands.
  • Underreporting: Many survivors of abuse in close-knit communities fear retaliation, ostracism, or disbelief, leading to significant underreporting of crimes.
  • Resource limitations: Many tribal law enforcement agencies operate with severely limited funding, staffing, and investigative resources compared to their state and federal counterparts.
  • Cultural manipulation: Perpetrators who embed themselves within cultural or spiritual frameworks can exploit community trust in ways that make it especially difficult for victims to speak out.

Survivor advocates attended the sentencing in large numbers, with many wearing orange, a color associated with awareness campaigns for missing and murdered Indigenous women. Several survivors delivered victim impact statements to the court, describing the lasting psychological, physical, and spiritual harm they had suffered as a result of Chasing Horse's actions.

Survivor Voices and Community Response

The sentencing hearing was an emotionally charged event, with survivors and their families filling the courtroom. Many described feeling that the legal system had finally listened to them after years of being dismissed or ignored.

"He told us that what he was doing was sacred. He made us believe that speaking out would bring harm to our families and our communities. Today, I feel like our voices were finally heard," one survivor said in her statement to the court.

Indigenous leaders and activists from across the country issued statements following the verdict, calling the sentence a step toward justice while emphasizing that systemic reform is still urgently needed to protect Indigenous women and children from future abusers who might seek to exploit similar positions of authority.

Legal Proceedings and Investigation Timeline

Chasing Horse was first arrested in January 2023 in Las Vegas, Nevada, following a coordinated investigation involving federal agents, tribal law enforcement, and state authorities. At the time of his arrest, investigators executed search warrants at multiple properties and seized evidence that would later be used at trial. He was subsequently charged under federal statutes, including the Violence Against Women Act provisions that extended jurisdiction over certain crimes committed on tribal lands.

His trial began in late 2025, with prosecutors presenting testimony from multiple survivors alongside documentary evidence, digital records, and witness accounts. The defense argued that the relationships in question were consensual, but the jury rejected those claims after deliberating for several days. Chasing Horse was convicted on all major counts and remanded into federal custody pending sentencing.

Implications for Indigenous Justice Reform

Legal scholars and Indigenous rights advocates are watching this case closely for its potential implications on future prosecutions involving crimes against Indigenous people. The successful federal prosecution, despite the many jurisdictional and evidentiary challenges involved, has been cited as a model for how interagency cooperation can lead to accountability in cases that might otherwise be dropped or go unpursued.

Calls have grown louder in recent months for increased federal investment in tribal law enforcement, expanded jurisdiction for tribal courts, and greater resources for survivors of violence in Indigenous communities. Legislation that would address some of these systemic gaps has been under discussion in Congress, and advocates hope that the high-profile nature of the Chasing Horse case will add momentum to those reform efforts.

For the communities most directly affected by his actions, the sentence represents a painful but necessary reckoning. Many survivors and their supporters expressed hope that the outcome would encourage other victims of abuse within Indigenous communities to come forward, and that it would serve as a warning to those who might seek to exploit positions of cultural or spiritual authority for criminal purposes.

Nathan Chasing Horse, once celebrated as a symbol of Indigenous representation in Hollywood, will now spend the better part of his remaining years behind bars. His minimum 37-year sentence means he will not be eligible for release until he is well into his eighties, a reality that many survivors described as a measure of justice, even as they acknowledged that no sentence could fully undo the damage he had caused.
