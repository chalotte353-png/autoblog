Artemis III Core Rocket Stage Arrives at Kennedy Space Center

NASA's Space Launch System core stage for the historic Artemis III mission has arrived at Kennedy Space Center in Florida, bringing humanity one step closer to returning astronauts to the lunar surface. The massive rocket component completed its journey from NASA's Michoud Assembly Facility in New Orleans aboard the Pegasus barge. Engineers and technicians are now preparing the stage for a series of critical inspections and integration procedures ahead of launch.

Space Correspondent
· May 01, 2026 · 5 min read

A Historic Delivery for Humanity's Return to the Moon

Kennedy Space Center in Florida received a momentous delivery on May 1, 2026, as the core stage of the Space Launch System rocket designated for the Artemis III mission arrived at the launch facility after a multi-day voyage from NASA's Michoud Assembly Facility in New Orleans, Louisiana. The towering rocket component, measuring approximately 212 feet in length and weighing nearly 100,000 pounds when empty, was transported aboard the Pegasus barge along the Gulf of Mexico and up through Florida's Banana River waterway before docking at the space center's turn basin wharf.

The arrival marks one of the most significant logistical milestones in NASA's Artemis program, which aims to land the first woman and the first person of color on the lunar surface. Artemis III is slated to be the mission that achieves this historic goal, following the crewed lunar orbital flight conducted during Artemis II.

What Is the Core Stage and Why Does It Matter?

The Space Launch System core stage is the backbone of NASA's most powerful rocket ever built. It houses four RS-25 engines that, when ignited, generate approximately 2 million pounds of thrust. Combined with two solid rocket boosters, the full SLS vehicle produces more than 8.8 million pounds of thrust at liftoff, making it capable of sending the Orion spacecraft and its crew beyond Earth's orbit and toward the Moon.

The core stage for Artemis III is the third such unit to be completed at Michoud, and engineers incorporated lessons learned from both the Artemis I uncrewed test flight and the Artemis II crewed mission into its construction. Key refinements include improvements to welding processes for the liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen propellant tanks, as well as enhanced thermal protection systems designed to improve reliability during the intense aerodynamic and thermal stresses of launch.

Components and Engineering Highlights

  • Four RS-25 Engines: Upgraded variants of the Space Shuttle Main Engine, each producing approximately 512,000 pounds of thrust at sea level.
  • Liquid Hydrogen Tank: The largest cryogenic propellant tank ever manufactured in the United States, holding more than 537,000 gallons of liquid hydrogen.
  • Liquid Oxygen Tank: Stores approximately 196,000 gallons of liquid oxygen used as oxidizer during ascent.
  • Intertank Structure: A structural component connecting the two propellant tanks, housing avionic systems and providing attachment points for the solid rocket boosters.
  • Engine Section: The aft portion of the vehicle that supports the four main engines and provides structural attachment for the mobile launcher.

The Journey from Michoud to Kennedy

The Pegasus barge, which has a storied history of transporting large NASA rocket components dating back to the Apollo program era, departed the Michoud Assembly Facility in late April 2026. The 310-foot-long vessel navigated the Gulf of Mexico before entering the Atlantic Intracoastal Waterway system and proceeding to Kennedy Space Center's specialized offloading facility.

Officials overseeing the transport noted that weather conditions along the route were favorable, allowing the journey to proceed on schedule. A small convoy of tugboats assisted the barge during the final approach to the wharf, where a dedicated crew of technicians and crane operators carefully secured the towering rocket stage for unloading.

"The arrival of the Artemis III core stage is a testament to the dedication and precision of the thousands of workers across our nation who have poured their expertise into building this rocket. We are on the path to history," said a senior NASA official during the welcoming ceremony held at the turn basin wharf.

Next Steps: Integration and Testing at the Vehicle Assembly Building

Following its arrival, the core stage will be transported to the Vehicle Assembly Building, one of the largest structures in the world by volume. There, technicians will conduct a comprehensive series of post-shipment inspections to verify the stage arrived without damage and that all systems remain within specification. These inspections are expected to take several weeks and involve detailed examination of welds, fluid lines, avionics harnesses, and thermal protection materials.

Once inspections are complete, the core stage will be lifted vertically and mated with the twin solid rocket boosters, which are being assembled from segments already stored at Kennedy Space Center. The Orion spacecraft and its European Service Module, currently undergoing final preparations at the Multi-Payload Processing Facility and the Neil Armstrong Operations and Checkout Building respectively, will subsequently be integrated atop the fully assembled SLS rocket.

Key Milestones Ahead Before Launch

  • Core stage post-shipment inspections and functional testing
  • Solid rocket booster stacking on the mobile launcher
  • Core stage lift and mating to the solid rocket boosters
  • Upper stage and Orion spacecraft integration
  • Wet dress rehearsal at Launch Complex 39B
  • Final pre-launch countdown preparations and launch readiness review

Artemis III Mission Profile

Artemis III is designed to land two astronauts on the lunar South Pole region, an area of significant scientific interest due to evidence of water ice deposits in permanently shadowed craters. These ice deposits could represent a critical in-situ resource for future sustained lunar exploration and even serve as a precursor to deep-space missions to Mars.

The mission will make use of SpaceX's Starship Human Landing System, which underwent extensive development and testing in prior years. Orion will carry the crew from Earth to lunar orbit, where two astronauts will transfer to the Starship lander for the descent to the surface. The landing crew is expected to spend approximately six to seven days on the lunar surface conducting scientific investigations, collecting samples, and deploying instruments before ascending back to Orion for the return journey to Earth.

The remaining two crew members aboard Orion will continue to conduct scientific observations from lunar orbit during the surface excursion phase of the mission.

A Nation and World Watching

The significance of the Artemis III mission extends well beyond the boundaries of Space Exploration. It represents a renewed commitment to scientific discovery, international cooperation, and the advancement of human potential. Dozens of nations and international space agencies have contributed to the broader Artemis program through the Artemis Accords, a set of bilateral agreements outlining principles for the peaceful and transparent exploration of the Moon.

Public interest in the mission continues to grow as launch approaches. Educational programs tied to the Artemis initiative have reached millions of students across the United States and globally, inspiring a new generation of scientists, engineers, and explorers who see the Moon not as a destination already visited, but as the next frontier waiting to be understood.

With the core stage now safely delivered and the integration process set to begin in the coming days, the countdown to one of the most momentous human spaceflight missions in decades has entered a tangible new phase. Kennedy Space Center, long the birthplace of humanity's most daring journeys into space, is once again preparing to serve as the launching point for history.

Looking Beyond Artemis III

NASA officials have also reaffirmed that Artemis III is not an endpoint but a beginning. Plans for Artemis IV and subsequent missions include the delivery and assembly of the Gateway lunar space station, which will serve as a permanent orbiting outpost to support long-duration lunar surface operations. Future missions envision crews spending weeks and eventually months on the lunar surface, laying the groundwork for permanent human habitation beyond Earth and the technological capabilities necessary for a crewed Mars mission in the 2030s and 2040s.

The arrival of the Artemis III core stage at Kennedy Space Center is, in every sense, a landmark moment. It is a physical manifestation of years of engineering work, national investment, and human ambition. As the rocket takes shape in the Vehicle Assembly Building in the months ahead, the world will watch with anticipation as humanity prepares once more to leave footprints on another world.
