DOJ Authorizes Firing Squads as Federal Execution Method

The Justice Department has formally authorized the use of firing squads as a permitted method of execution in federal capital punishment cases. The move marks a significant escalation in the Trump administration's effort to accelerate and expand the use of the death penalty across the United States. Legal advocates and civil rights organizations have already signaled their intent to challenge the policy in federal court.

Justice Department Formally Adopts Firing Squad for Federal Executions

The United States Justice Department announced on April 26, 2026, that it will formally permit the use of firing squads as an approved method of execution for federal death row inmates. The decision represents one of the most significant expansions of federal execution protocols in decades and underscores the Trump Administration's aggressive posture on capital punishment as a cornerstone of its law and order agenda.

The announcement, delivered through an official departmental directive, states that Firing Squads will be listed alongside lethal injection and other existing methods as a lawful means of carrying out federal death sentences. Officials framed the change as a practical necessity, citing ongoing difficulties in obtaining the pharmaceutical compounds required for lethal injection procedures.

Administration Cites Drug Shortages and Legal Delays

Federal officials have long struggled to secure adequate supplies of execution drugs from pharmaceutical manufacturers, many of whom have explicitly prohibited the use of their products in capital punishment procedures. The Justice Department argues that broadening the roster of approved execution methods will reduce legal bottlenecks and allow the federal government to carry out sentences that have, in some cases, languished for years or even decades.

A senior Justice Department official stated in a prepared release:

"The federal government has a solemn obligation to ensure that justice is carried out for victims of the most heinous crimes. Bureaucratic and logistical obstacles should not stand in the way of lawfully imposed sentences. This policy change ensures that the Department has the tools it needs to fulfill its constitutional responsibilities."

The administration has repeatedly characterized delays in carrying out executions as a failure of the justice system and has made expediting capital cases a visible priority since taking office.

What the New Policy Entails

Under the updated protocol, federal authorities will be permitted to use a firing squad when other methods are deemed unavailable or have been legally challenged. The specific procedural guidelines are expected to be finalized in the coming weeks, but the directive establishes the legal and administrative foundation for its use.

Key elements of the announced policy include:

  • Expanded method options: Firing squads will be formally added to the list of federally sanctioned execution methods, providing an alternative when lethal injection drugs are unavailable.
  • Expedited review timelines: The Justice Department is simultaneously pushing for tighter timelines on last-minute legal appeals, arguing that prolonged review processes have created unconstitutional delays in carrying out lawful sentences.
  • State coordination: Federal officials indicated they will work with states that already permit firing squads to potentially share infrastructure and operational expertise.
  • Inmate notification requirements: Condemned individuals will reportedly be informed in advance of the method to be used, though civil liberties groups argue the notification window being proposed is insufficient.

Legal Challenges Expected

The announcement has drawn immediate condemnation from civil liberties organizations, death penalty abolition advocates, and a number of legal scholars who argue the policy raises serious constitutional questions under the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment.

Legal advocacy groups have indicated they are prepared to file federal court challenges as soon as the procedural guidelines are formally published. Critics argue that expanding execution methods rather than addressing the underlying question of whether the federal death penalty is applied fairly and accurately is the wrong direction for the country.

Several prominent defense attorneys issued a joint statement asserting that the administration's push to accelerate executions increases the risk of executing innocent individuals, a concern amplified by the growing body of exoneration cases in capital convictions over the past three decades.

Historical Use of Firing Squads

The firing squad is one of the oldest methods of execution in American history, though its use has declined sharply over the twentieth century. A small number of states, including Utah and South Carolina, have enacted or maintained statutes authorizing Firing Squads as an alternative or backup execution method. At the federal level, however, the method has not been formally adopted in the modern era of capital punishment.

Proponents of the method, including some who oppose lethal injection on humanitarian grounds, have argued that a properly administered firing squad can result in a faster and potentially less painful death than a botched lethal injection. Several high-profile lethal injection executions in recent years drew intense public scrutiny after inmates appeared to suffer prolonged deaths.

Political Context and Administration Priorities

The Trump Administration's move to authorize firing squads fits within a broader policy agenda that has placed significant emphasis on expanding and accelerating federal capital punishment. Since returning to office, the administration has reinstated and scheduled executions for numerous inmates on federal death row and has publicly championed the death penalty as a deterrent against violent crime and terrorism.

The President and senior administration officials have repeatedly called for the death penalty to be applied more swiftly and to a broader category of crimes, including certain drug trafficking offenses. While legislative efforts to formally expand the scope of capital punishment have faced obstacles in Congress, the administration has used executive and departmental authority to push the boundaries of existing law wherever possible.

Reaction from Lawmakers

Reaction from Capitol Hill was sharply divided along partisan lines. Several Republican lawmakers praised the Justice Department's decision, calling it a long-overdue step toward ensuring that the most serious criminals face the ultimate consequence for their actions. Democratic members of Congress condemned the move, with some calling for congressional hearings and others vowing to pursue legislative remedies to block the policy's implementation.

A Democratic senator from a northeastern state described the announcement as "a retreat to a darker chapter in American history" and called on colleagues on both sides of the aisle to scrutinize the administration's broader capital punishment agenda more carefully.

What Comes Next

The Justice Department is expected to publish formal procedural regulations in the Federal Register in the weeks ahead, opening a public comment period as required under administrative law. Legal challenges are virtually certain to follow, and the policy's ultimate fate may rest with the federal judiciary, including potentially the Supreme Court.

Death penalty observers note that the Supreme Court's current composition makes it difficult to predict how the justices might rule on Eighth Amendment challenges to the expanded protocol, particularly given the court's recent reluctance to broadly revisit capital punishment jurisprudence.

As of the date of this report, no specific execution dates have been announced under the new firing squad authorization, but federal officials have made clear that the groundwork is being laid to move forward as quickly as logistical and legal circumstances permit.

The coming months are expected to produce significant courtroom battles over not only the firing squad policy itself but also the administration's wider effort to accelerate and expand the use of capital punishment at the federal level, a debate that touches on some of the most fundamental questions about justice, human dignity, and the role of the state in American democracy.
