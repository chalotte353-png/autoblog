Trump's Approval Rating Sinks to 37% Amid Prolonged Iran Conflict

President Donald Trump's job approval rating has dropped to a troubling 37%, according to a new NBC News poll released Tuesday, April 21, 2026, as the United States continues to grapple with a deeply unpopular military conflict with Iran and pursues an uncertain path toward a negotiated peace settlement. The survey, which polled a national sample of American adults, underscores the significant domestic political costs of a war that has divided the country along party, generational, and geographic lines.

The findings come at a pivotal moment for the Trump administration, which has been engaged in back-channel and direct diplomatic talks aimed at ending hostilities that have stretched well beyond initial White House projections. Senior officials have signaled cautious optimism about the peace process, but the latest polling data suggests that much of the American public remains deeply skeptical — and increasingly exhausted.

What the Poll Found

The NBC News survey, conducted April 17–19, 2026, among 1,000 adults nationwide, painted a stark picture of public sentiment toward both the administration and the conflict itself:

  • 37% of U.S. adults approve of Trump's overall job performance, while 58% disapprove — a gap that has widened by six percentage points since January.
  • Only 29% of respondents said they support continued U.S. military involvement in Iran, down from 41% when the conflict escalated in late 2025.
  • 64% of Americans said the conflict has not been worth the cost in lives and resources.
  • Among independents — a critical voting bloc — Trump's approval sits at just 31%, a record low for his current term.
  • Even among Republican respondents, approval of Trump's Iran policy has slipped to 68%, down from a high of 79% in December 2025.

The NBC News poll carries a margin of error of plus or minus 3.1 percentage points.

A Nation Weary of War

The political dynamics surrounding the Iran conflict have grown increasingly complicated for an administration that initially framed military action as a swift and decisive operation. In the months since hostilities began, the conflict has resisted easy resolution, drawing the U.S. into a protracted engagement that has resulted in significant military casualties, billions of dollars in expenditure, and heightened tensions across the broader Middle East region.

Military analysts and foreign policy experts have noted that the conflict has followed a pattern familiar from previous U.S. engagements in the region — early public support followed by a rapid erosion of confidence as the mission's complexity became apparent.

Economic Anxiety Adding to Discontent

War fatigue is not the only driver of Trump's declining numbers. The NBC News poll also found that 61% of Americans believe the Iran conflict has contributed to rising fuel prices and broader economic instability. Oil markets have remained volatile throughout the engagement, with crude prices fluctuating sharply based on battlefield developments and diplomatic signals.

"The economic pain is real and it's tangible," said Dr. Miriam Okafor, a political economist at Georgetown University. "When Americans feel it at the gas pump and at the grocery store, they connect those dots back to presidential decision-making. That's reflected very clearly in these numbers."

The administration has pushed back on the characterization that the conflict is the primary driver of inflation, pointing to global supply chain dynamics and Federal Reserve policy. However, critics argue that the White House has struggled to build a compelling economic counter-narrative.

The Peace Negotiation Gamble

Against this backdrop of declining support, the Trump administration has signaled that a peace framework with Iran may be within reach. Secretary of State Marco Rubio indicated last week that talks mediated through Omani and Swiss diplomatic channels had entered "a serious and substantive phase," though he cautioned that significant obstacles remain.

White House Press Secretary Taylor Budowich told reporters on Monday that President Trump remains "fully committed to achieving a durable peace that protects American interests and ensures regional stability," adding that the administration would not accept a deal that leaves Iran with a viable nuclear weapons program.

Iran's foreign ministry has issued mixed signals, with some officials expressing openness to a ceasefire framework while hardliners within the Revolutionary Guard have publicly opposed any agreement that constrains Iran's military posture.

Congressional Pressure Mounting

On Capitol Hill, pressure on the administration is building from both parties. A bipartisan group of senators introduced legislation last week that would require congressional approval for any significant escalation of military operations in Iran, reviving longstanding debates about the limits of presidential war powers.

"The American people are not behind this war, and Congress has an obligation to reassert its constitutional role," said Senator Tim Kaine (D-VA), one of the bill's co-sponsors. Several moderate Republican senators have also expressed concern, though leadership has resisted calling for a floor vote.

For the Trump White House, the congressional dynamics add another layer of complexity to an already difficult political environment. Administration officials have argued that existing authorizations provide sufficient legal cover for current operations, but legal scholars remain divided on that interpretation.

Historical Context and Comparisons

Trump's 37% approval rating places him in historically troubled territory. For comparison, President George W. Bush's approval rating fell to 37% in mid-2006, during a period of severe public disillusionment with the Iraq War. President Lyndon B. Johnson saw his numbers collapse to similar levels in 1968 due to Vietnam. Both historical precedents underline the degree to which protracted, unpopular military engagements can define — and ultimately damage — a presidency.

"History tells us that when a president's approval falls into the mid-to-low thirties during a military conflict, recovery is possible but extremely difficult," said Professor James Holbrooke, a presidential historian at the University of Virginia. "It typically requires either a dramatic diplomatic breakthrough or a fundamental shift in the military situation on the ground."

The Road Ahead

With peace talks ongoing and no clear timeline for resolution, the political calculus for the Trump administration remains fraught. A successful negotiated settlement could provide a significant boost to presidential standing, potentially reframing the narrative around decisive leadership. However, a failed negotiation — or a return to active hostilities — could deepen an already serious approval crisis.

Democratic strategists have already begun incorporating the war's unpopularity into their messaging frameworks for the 2026 midterm elections, scheduled for November. Several vulnerable Republican incumbents in swing districts have reportedly expressed private concern about running in an environment defined by an unpopular foreign conflict and economic anxiety.

For now, the White House is focused on projecting confidence about the peace process while managing an increasingly restless public and a nervous congressional coalition. Whether that strategy will prove sufficient to reverse the administration's political fortunes remains an open and consequential question.

The NBC News poll results serve as a clear data point in what has become one of the defining political stories of Trump's second term: the growing gap between an administration's stated objectives abroad and the patience of an American public bearing the costs at home.
