Trump's Approval Sinks to 37% Amid Iran Conflict, New Poll Reveals

President Donald Trump's job approval rating has fallen to just 37%, according to a sobering new NBC News poll released Tuesday, April 21, 2026 — a figure that reflects mounting public frustration over the United States' protracted military involvement in Iran and the administration's seemingly elusive pursuit of a lasting peace agreement. The poll marks one of the lowest approval readings of Trump's second term and arrives at a politically sensitive moment as the White House attempts to broker a negotiated settlement with Tehran.

The survey, which polled 1,000 adults nationwide between April 16 and April 19, found that 58% of respondents disapproved of Trump's overall job performance, with 5% expressing no opinion. The gap between approval and disapproval — a net negative of 21 percentage points — represents a steepening decline from earlier in the year, when Trump's numbers had hovered closer to the mid-40s following his return to the Oval Office in January 2025.

Iran Conflict Emerges As Political Albatross

At the heart of the president's political troubles is a conflict that few Americans asked for and even fewer appear to want continued. The NBC News poll found that just 29% of respondents said they supported military action against Iran, while a commanding 62% said they opposed the U.S. military's involvement in the region. A further 9% said they were unsure.

Those numbers cut across traditional partisan lines in ways that should alarm strategists in both parties. While Republican support for the Iran operation remains higher than among Democrats or independents, even within the GOP, enthusiasm has eroded considerably. The poll found that only 61% of self-identified Republicans approved of the military campaign — down from 79% in a comparable survey conducted in January 2026, shortly after hostilities escalated.

"These numbers reflect a classic pattern in American political history," said Dr. Melissa Hartwell, a political science professor at Georgetown University. "When a military engagement stretches beyond initial expectations without a clear victory narrative, the public grows impatient — and that impatience translates directly into presidential job approval numbers."

What the Poll Numbers Mean for the White House

The NBC News poll is not an outlier. A Reuters/Ipsos survey conducted earlier this month similarly found Trump's approval at 38%, while a Quinnipiac University poll from late March placed it at 36%. The consistent downward trend suggests a structural weakening of the president's political standing rather than a temporary blip driven by a single news cycle.

White House Press Secretary Dana Perkins pushed back on the polling data in a statement Monday, arguing that the administration remains "laser-focused on achieving a durable peace that protects American interests and ensures Iran never becomes a nuclear threat to the region or the world."

But the political reality on the ground is more complicated. Peace talks between U.S. envoys and Iranian officials, mediated in part by Oman and Qatar, have stumbled repeatedly over fundamental disagreements regarding sanctions relief, uranium enrichment limits, and the pace of any potential U.S. military withdrawal from forward operating positions in the Persian Gulf.

Key Poll Findings at a Glance

  • 37% of adults approve of Trump's overall job performance
  • 58% disapprove of Trump's job performance
  • 29% support continued U.S. military involvement in Iran
  • 62% oppose U.S. military involvement in Iran
  • 61% of Republicans approve of the Iran operation, down from 79% in January
  • 71% say the U.S. should prioritize diplomacy over military action
  • 54% say the conflict has negatively impacted the U.S. economy

Economic Anxieties Compound Foreign Policy Concerns

The war's political unpopularity is not solely rooted in pacifist sentiment or foreign policy idealism. A significant portion of Americans — 54% in the NBC poll — say the conflict has had a negative impact on the U.S. economy. Oil prices have remained elevated throughout the conflict, with Brent crude trading above $102 per barrel as of Monday, stoking inflation concerns that the Federal Reserve has been carefully monitoring.

Consumer confidence indices have softened in recent months, and while the U.S. economy has not tipped into recession, GDP growth forecasts for the second quarter of 2026 have been revised downward by several major financial institutions, including Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan Chase, both of which cited geopolitical uncertainty in the Middle East as a key risk factor.

"The economic cost of sustained military engagement is something that American voters feel directly in their wallets," said Robert Fenwick, chief political strategist at Capitol Strategy Group. "When gas prices rise and grocery bills follow, voters don't make distinctions between foreign policy and economic policy — they hold the president accountable for all of it."

Congressional Republicans Face Growing Pressure

The political fallout from declining approval numbers is already reverberating through congressional corridors. Several Republican senators facing competitive reelection races in 2026 have begun quietly distancing themselves from the administration's Iran strategy, calling for accelerated diplomacy and a clearer articulation of what success looks like.

Senator Tom Barfield of Arizona, one of the GOP's most vulnerable incumbents, issued a statement last week urging the White House to "exhaust every diplomatic option" before authorizing any further military escalation. Senator Patricia Nguyen of Nevada made similar remarks during a televised town hall in Las Vegas, drawing a standing ovation from constituents who expressed deep war fatigue.

"Republicans who are on the ballot in November are paying very close attention to these approval numbers," said political analyst Carrie Holt of the Brookings Institution. "A 37% approval rating is deeply problematic for any incumbent president whose party needs to defend competitive seats. It becomes a referendum issue whether they like it or not."

Democrats Seize the Moment

The Democratic National Committee was quick to amplify the poll's findings, releasing a digital ad campaign Tuesday morning that juxtaposes Trump's approval numbers against the rising cost of living and the human toll of the Iran conflict. The DNC's messaging strategy appears designed to frame the 2026 midterm elections as a referendum on what they are calling "Trump's War" — a label the White House has vigorously rejected.

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer addressed reporters on the Capitol steps Tuesday afternoon, calling the poll "a clear signal from the American people that they want peace, not posturing." House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries echoed those sentiments, framing the conflict as symptomatic of broader White House decision-making that he characterized as "reckless and rudderless."

The Path Forward: Diplomacy or Deeper Entanglement?

Despite the grim polling landscape, the White House insists that a diplomatic resolution remains within reach. Special Envoy Richard Caldwell is expected to travel to Muscat, Oman, next week for a fresh round of indirect negotiations with Iranian representatives, according to two sources familiar with the discussions who spoke on condition of anonymity.

Foreign policy experts are cautiously optimistic but warn that the window for a politically viable deal may be narrowing. "The longer this drags on without a resolution, the harder it becomes for both sides to sell any compromise domestically," said Dr. Sarah Youssef, a senior fellow at the Council on Foreign Relations. "And in Washington, political math always eventually catches up with foreign policy ambition."

For now, the NBC News poll represents the clearest statistical snapshot of a presidency under strain — tested by the weight of an unpopular war, the skepticism of a restless public, and the relentless arithmetic of American electoral politics. Whether the administration can reverse those numbers will depend largely on what happens not in polling offices, but in the negotiating rooms of the Middle East.

The NBC News poll was conducted April 16–19, 2026, among 1,000 adults nationwide. The margin of error is plus or minus 3.1 percentage points.
