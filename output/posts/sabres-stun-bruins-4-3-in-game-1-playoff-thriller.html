Sabres Stun Bruins 4-3 in Game 1 Playoff Thriller

The Buffalo Sabres erased a two-goal deficit in the final period to defeat the Boston Bruins 4-3 in a dramatic Game 1 on Monday night. Tage Thompson and Mattias Samuelsson were the architects of a furious comeback that sent TD Garden into stunned silence. Buffalo now holds a 1-0 series lead heading into Game 2.

Sabres Engineer Stunning Comeback to Steal Game 1 from Bruins

BOSTON — In a performance that will be etched into Buffalo Sabres playoff lore for years to come, Tage Thompson and Mattias Samuelsson orchestrated one of the most breathtaking comebacks of the 2026 NHL postseason, lifting the Sabres to a 4-3 victory over the Boston Bruins in Game 1 of their first-round series on Monday night at TD Garden.

Down by two goals entering the third period and facing a hostile crowd that had written Buffalo's obituary in ink, the Sabres refused to yield. In a 14-minute span of electrifying hockey, they turned a seemingly comfortable Bruins lead into a stunning defeat, claiming the opening game of the series and sending a clear message that this is no ordinary Buffalo team.

A Slow Start Gives Way to Brilliance

For much of the first two periods, it appeared the Bruins would cruise to a comfortable home victory. Boston built a 3-1 lead thanks to sharp, disciplined play and the energetic backing of a raucous TD Garden crowd, which had been starved for playoff hockey of this caliber.

The Bruins were methodical and physical in the opening frame, imposing their style on a Sabres squad that looked hesitant and rattled in the early going. Boston's forecheck pinned Buffalo deep in their own zone for extended stretches, and their power play converted on one of two opportunities, reminding the hockey world why this franchise remains one of the Eastern Conference's most dangerous outfits come April.

Thompson, Buffalo's franchise cornerstone and one of the most dominant centers in the league, struggled to find his rhythm through 40 minutes. His linemates worked hard but couldn't generate the sustained offensive pressure the Sabres needed. At the second intermission, the mood in the visiting locker room had to be recalibrated entirely.

Thompson Ignites the Comeback

Whatever was said between periods had a transformative effect. The Sabres came out for the third period as a different team — urgent, precise, and utterly relentless.

Thompson broke through first, converting at 6:42 of the third period with a devastating wrist shot from the left circle that beat Bruins goaltender Jeremy Swayman clean to the glove side. The goal immediately shifted the energy in the building, silencing portions of the crowd and invigorating a Buffalo bench that had grown restless.

"We just needed one," Thompson said after the game. "When you get that first one, you believe. And once we believed, I don't think anyone was going to stop us tonight."

Thompson, who finished the night with a goal and two assists, was the kind of transformational player his supporters always envisioned when Buffalo selected him. His skating was electric in the third period, his positioning impeccable, and his shot — always a weapon — was on full display against one of the Eastern Conference's top goaltenders.

Samuelsson Delivers the Equalizer

With the TD Garden crowd growing increasingly anxious, defenseman Mattias Samuelsson delivered the goal that knotted the game at three. At 11:09 of the third period, Samuelsson stepped into a heavy slap shot from the right point, finding a lane through a tangle of bodies and beating Swayman low on the blocker side.

It was the kind of contribution that defines playoff hockey — an unexpected hero rising to the moment when the franchise needed it most. Samuelsson, who has evolved into one of Buffalo's most reliable blueliners over the past two seasons, pumped both fists and roared toward the Buffalo bench as his teammates swarmed him.

"Matty's been unbelievable for us all year," said Sabres head coach Lindy Ruff. "For him to step up in that moment, in this building, that's a veteran play. That's a player who wanted it."

The Game-Winner and Series Implications

With momentum firmly in Buffalo's favor and TD Garden operating on pure nerves, Thompson orchestrated the game-winning sequence with 4:31 remaining in regulation. He threaded a pass through two defenders to find Jason Zucker charging down the left wing, and Zucker buried his shot past a sprawling Swayman to give the Sabres their first lead of the night.

Buffalo's goaltender Ukko-Pekka Luukkonen was equal to the challenge in the final minutes, turning aside a desperate Bruins push with several key saves, including a remarkable left pad stop on David Pastrnak with 90 seconds remaining that preserved the one-goal advantage and sent the Sabres bench into a frenzy.

The final buzzer triggered an outpouring of emotion on the Buffalo side — a franchise that has endured more than its share of playoff heartbreak finally exhaling after a pressure-filled Game 1 performance.

Key Statistics from Game 1

  • Tage Thompson: 1 goal, 2 assists, 7 shots on goal, plus-2 rating
  • Mattias Samuelsson: 1 goal, 1 assist, 4 blocked shots
  • Ukko-Pekka Luukkonen: 31 saves on 34 shots
  • Jeremy Swayman: 29 saves on 33 shots
  • Buffalo power play: 1-for-3
  • Boston power play: 1-for-4

Bruins Look to Regroup

For Boston, the defeat was a bitter pill. The Bruins controlled the majority of the game and had every reason to believe they would close it out, only to surrender the lead in a seven-minute span that will haunt them until Game 2.

Pastrnak led Boston with a goal and an assist, while Brad Marchand and Charlie McAvoy each contributed points. But their failure to protect a two-goal lead at home will be the defining narrative heading into Wednesday evening.

"That's on us," Bruins captain Marchand said bluntly in the post-game corridor. "We had that game. They competed harder than us when it mattered most. That can't happen again."

Boston head coach Jim Montgomery was measured but direct in his assessment. "We gave them life and they took it. Buffalo is a good team, and we need to be sharper in every detail. One game doesn't define a series, but we have to be better."

A Historic Moment for Buffalo

The victory carries enormous symbolic weight for a franchise that last appeared in the playoffs years ago, a drought that tested the patience of one of the NHL's most passionate fan bases. The city of Buffalo erupted in celebration following the final buzzer, with social media awash in outpourings from a fanbase that has waited a long time for a night like this.

"This is just the beginning," Thompson said with a wide grin. "We're not satisfied with one game. We want to win a series."

Game 2 of the series is scheduled for Wednesday, April 23, at TD Garden, with puck drop set for 7:00 p.m. ET.
