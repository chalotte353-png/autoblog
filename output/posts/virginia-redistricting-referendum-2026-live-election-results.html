Virginia Redistricting Referendum 2026: What You Need to Know

Virginia voters are deciding the fate of a sweeping redistricting referendum on April 23, 2026, in a special election that has captured national attention and sparked fierce debate across the commonwealth. The ballot measure, known officially as the Virginia Fair Maps Amendment, asks voters whether to overhaul the existing process for drawing state legislative and congressional district boundaries following concerns raised by lawmakers, advocacy groups, and members of the public about partisan influence in mapmaking.

Polls opened at 6:00 a.m. across Virginia's 133 localities, with election officials reporting steady turnout throughout the day in key population centers including Northern Virginia, the Hampton Roads region, and the Richmond metropolitan area. Results are expected to roll in throughout the evening as precincts across the state finalize their counts.

Live Results: Vote Totals as Precincts Report

As of the most recent update on the evening of April 23, 2026, results are coming in from across Virginia's 95 counties and 38 independent cities. Early returns suggest a competitive race, with urban and suburban areas showing strong support for the amendment while more rural precincts have leaned against the measure.

Election officials have cautioned that absentee and mail-in ballots, which were cast in record numbers ahead of the special election, may take additional time to be fully counted and incorporated into the final tally. Virginia law permits absentee ballots postmarked by Election Day to be counted if received within three days of the election.

Key Counties to Watch

  • Fairfax County: The commonwealth's most populous jurisdiction is reporting high turnout, with early results showing strong support for the redistricting amendment among Northern Virginia suburban voters.
  • Chesterfield County: This fast-growing Richmond suburb has been a bellwether in recent Virginia elections. Returns here could indicate whether the measure has crossover appeal among independent and moderate voters.
  • Virginia Beach: The state's largest city by population is considered a competitive battleground, and its results are being watched closely as a possible indicator of the statewide outcome.
  • Augusta County: This rural Shenandoah Valley county is among those reporting early resistance to the amendment, reflecting a pattern seen in several rural western Virginia localities.
  • Prince William County: Another rapidly growing Northern Virginia suburb that has trended more competitive in recent cycles, with early returns being closely analyzed by both campaigns.

What the Referendum Would Change

At the heart of the debate is a proposed restructuring of the Virginia Redistricting Commission, the bipartisan body established by a 2020 constitutional amendment to take mapmaking authority away from the General Assembly. Supporters of the new referendum argue that the existing commission has fallen short of its original promise, with critics pointing to several rounds of gridlock and court-ordered maps that they say still reflect partisan interests.

The 2026 referendum, if passed, would implement several significant changes to how Virginia draws its district lines:

  • Replace the current citizen-legislative hybrid commission with an independent citizens-only panel drawn from a pool of applicants screened for conflicts of interest
  • Establish new criteria prioritizing communities of interest and competitive districts alongside traditional factors such as population equality and geographic compactness
  • Require greater transparency in the mapmaking process, including mandatory public hearings in every congressional district
  • Create a dedicated legal fund to defend adopted maps in court, reducing reliance on General Assembly appropriations

Supporters Make Their Case

Advocates for the measure have spent months building a coalition that spans the ideological spectrum, arguing that truly independent redistricting is essential to restoring public trust in Virginia's democratic institutions.

Virginians deserve maps drawn by the people, not by politicians protecting their own seats. This referendum is a once-in-a-generation opportunity to put fairness first and let voters choose their representatives rather than the other way around.

The pro-amendment campaign raised substantial funds from good-government organizations, civic groups, and individual donors across the state. Endorsements came from a wide range of voices including former governors, retired judges, and local elected officials from both major parties who argued that the current system remains too susceptible to manipulation.

Opposition Arguments

Opponents of the referendum have raised concerns about accountability, arguing that removing elected officials entirely from the mapmaking process could create an unelected body with enormous power and insufficient public oversight.

This amendment would hand the keys to Virginia's democracy to an unaccountable panel of political appointees with no obligation to answer to voters. The existing commission, while imperfect, at least ensures that the people's elected representatives have a seat at the table.

Critics have also questioned the selection process for the proposed independent commission, arguing that screening mechanisms could introduce their own biases and that the practical effect might be a body skewed toward particular ideological or regional interests.

Turnout and Voting Trends

Virginia election officials reported brisk turnout at polling locations throughout the day, particularly in the densely populated Interstate 95 corridor stretching from the Washington suburbs south through Richmond and toward Hampton Roads. Several precincts in Northern Virginia reported lines at peak hours, prompting local officials to extend staffing levels.

Absentee voting ahead of the special election reached historically high levels for an off-cycle referendum, with the Virginia Department of Elections confirming that more than 340,000 absentee ballots were requested and the vast majority returned before Election Day. That figure represents a significant increase compared to previous special elections held outside of a general election cycle.

Voter advocacy groups attributed the elevated pre-election participation to an extensive outreach campaign that targeted infrequent voters, young adults, and communities that have historically seen lower participation rates in non-November elections.

National Significance

While Virginia's redistricting referendum is a state-level ballot measure, election observers and political analysts across the country have been following the race closely. Virginia has increasingly been viewed as a testing ground for democratic reform initiatives, and the outcome of this referendum is expected to influence similar efforts already underway in several other states.

Several national organizations with stakes in redistricting policy have deployed resources to Virginia in recent weeks, both in support of and in opposition to the measure. The results tonight are anticipated to send a signal about voter appetite for redistricting reform heading into the 2028 cycle, when congressional and state legislative maps drawn after the 2030 census will once again be subject to intense scrutiny.

What Happens Next

If the referendum passes, the Virginia General Assembly would be required to enact enabling legislation within 12 months establishing the new independent commission and outlining its operational procedures. Legal challenges are widely anticipated regardless of the outcome, and election law experts have noted that the new amendment's language has already been scrutinized by attorneys on both sides.

If the measure fails, the current redistricting commission structure established by the 2020 amendment would remain in place. However, supporters of reform have indicated they would continue to pursue legislative and legal avenues to address what they describe as persistent flaws in the current process.

Election officials expect a majority of votes to be reported by midnight, though the final certified results including all absentee and provisional ballots may not be available for several additional days. Virginia residents and observers nationwide are encouraged to follow official state election authority updates for the most accurate and timely information as results continue to be tabulated throughout the night.
