US Stablecoins Threaten Emerging Markets, Central Banks Warn

Central bankers from emerging market economies are sounding the alarm over the rapid proliferation of US dollar-pegged stablecoins, warning that the trend could accelerate dollarisation and undermine monetary sovereignty. The concerns have intensified as the Trump administration actively promotes stablecoin adoption as part of a broader pro-crypto agenda. Regulators fear that without coordinated international oversight, these digital assets could destabilise fragile financial systems and enable widespread criminal misuse.

Central Bankers Sound Alarm Over Dollar-Pegged Crypto Assets

Senior monetary policymakers from across the developing world are raising urgent concerns about the accelerating growth of US dollar-pegged stablecoins, arguing that these digital assets pose a significant and underappreciated threat to the financial stability of emerging market economies. The warnings come as Washington embraces a markedly pro-crypto posture under President Donald Trump, whose administration has moved aggressively to promote stablecoin adoption both domestically and internationally.

Speaking at an international monetary policy forum in Basel this week, central bank governors and senior officials from nations including Brazil, Nigeria, Indonesia, and South Africa expressed alarm that the expanding footprint of stablecoins could erode their ability to control monetary conditions, manage exchange rates, and enforce capital flow regulations. Several officials described the situation as one of the most pressing cross-border financial stability challenges of the current decade.

The Dollarisation Threat

At the heart of the concern lies the risk of accelerated dollarisation, a process by which citizens and businesses in a country increasingly substitute the local currency with the US dollar for everyday transactions, savings, and contracts. Historically, dollarisation has occurred gradually in economies experiencing chronic inflation or political instability. Central bankers now warn that stablecoins could dramatically compress that timeline.

Unlike physical dollar bills, which require physical presence and bank access to circulate, stablecoins can be transferred instantly via smartphone to anyone with internet access. This frictionless accessibility means that citizens in countries with volatile currencies can now effectively park their savings in digital dollars with minimal effort, bypassing local banking systems entirely.

"When a citizen of our country moves their savings into a dollar-pegged digital token on a foreign blockchain, they are not just protecting their wealth. They are removing capital from our financial system, reducing the effectiveness of our monetary policy, and creating dependencies on infrastructure we do not regulate or control," said one senior central bank official who asked not to be named.

Economists estimate that in several high-inflation economies across Latin America and sub-Saharan Africa, stablecoin holdings now represent a measurable share of effective money supply. In some cases, peer-to-peer stablecoin transactions have begun to rival formal remittance channels in volume, raising concerns about the invisibility of these flows to national financial authorities.

Trump Administration's Pro-Stablecoin Push

The Trump Administration has positioned US dollar stablecoins as a powerful tool for extending the global reach of the dollar at a time when some observers have speculated about long-term threats to dollar dominance. Senior Treasury officials have argued that enabling a new generation of dollar-denominated digital assets strengthens American financial influence and provides a competitive advantage over rival payment systems, including those developed by China.

Legislative momentum in Washington has reinforced this direction. Proposed federal legislation aimed at creating a regulatory framework for stablecoins would formally legitimise major issuers and potentially open the door to broader international use. Proponents argue that regulated stablecoins could improve financial inclusion by giving unbanked populations access to stable, dollar-denominated digital money.

However, critics both inside and outside the United States contend that the domestic political calculus behind stablecoin promotion pays insufficient attention to the spillover effects on the rest of the world. Central bankers in Emerging Markets argue they were not adequately consulted during the drafting of proposed US stablecoin legislation and that its global implications have been largely ignored in Washington's internal debate.

Criminal Misuse and Regulatory Gaps

Beyond the macroeconomic concerns, law enforcement and financial intelligence officials have highlighted the potential for stablecoins to facilitate money laundering, sanctions evasion, and other financial crimes. The pseudonymous nature of many blockchain transactions, combined with the regulatory arbitrage created by issuers operating from loosely supervised jurisdictions, creates significant vulnerabilities.

Several emerging market governments have reported increases in stablecoin-related transactions connected to illicit trade, tax evasion, and cross-border capital flight. Financial intelligence units in multiple countries have noted that criminal networks are increasingly routing proceeds through stablecoin platforms precisely because oversight remains fragmented and inconsistent across borders.

  • Capital flight risk: Wealthy individuals and corporations using stablecoins to move assets offshore without triggering formal reporting requirements.
  • Sanctions evasion: Entities subject to international sanctions exploiting stablecoin infrastructure to access dollar-denominated markets indirectly.
  • Tax avoidance: Businesses settling transactions in stablecoins to obscure revenues from domestic tax authorities.
  • Illicit trade financing: Criminal networks using stablecoin transfers to move value across borders with reduced detection risk.

The Financial Action Task Force, the global standard-setter for anti-money laundering rules, has repeatedly flagged the inadequacy of current supervision over stablecoin issuers and exchanges. Despite updated guidance, implementation across jurisdictions remains uneven, leaving significant gaps that sophisticated actors can exploit.

Calls for International Coordination

Emerging market central bankers are pressing for a coordinated multilateral response, arguing that the risks posed by dollar stablecoins cannot be addressed effectively through unilateral national measures alone. Several officials have called for the International Monetary Fund and the Bank for International Settlements to take a more assertive role in developing binding international standards for stablecoin issuers, particularly those whose tokens circulate primarily outside the United States.

Proposed Measures Under Discussion

Among the proposals being floated in international policy circles are requirements for stablecoin issuers to register with and provide data to the central banks of jurisdictions where their tokens circulate above defined thresholds. Other suggestions include mandatory interoperability with national digital currency infrastructure and caps on stablecoin holdings by residents of countries that have not granted formal regulatory recognition to specific issuers.

Some officials have also called on the US Treasury and the Federal Reserve to engage bilaterally with affected central banks, providing technical assistance and information sharing to help emerging market regulators better monitor and manage stablecoin flows within their borders. So far, the response from Washington on this specific dimension has been limited.

The Tension Between Inclusion and Sovereignty

Not all policymakers in Emerging Markets take an entirely negative view of stablecoins. Some central bankers acknowledge that dollar-pegged digital assets can serve genuinely useful functions, particularly for remittances and financial inclusion in communities underserved by traditional banking. The challenge, they argue, is finding a framework that captures these benefits while protecting monetary sovereignty and preventing criminal misuse.

"We are not trying to prevent our citizens from accessing modern financial tools," one African central bank deputy governor explained. "We are asking that the global community help us ensure those tools do not hollow out our financial systems or become conduits for illicit money."

The Road Ahead

The debate over stablecoins and their international consequences is expected to feature prominently at upcoming meetings of the G20 and the IMF's International Monetary and Financial Committee later this year. Emerging market economies are expected to push for formal agenda space on the issue, seeking commitments from advanced economies to consider the cross-border impact of their domestic crypto regulatory decisions.

For now, the proliferation of dollar stablecoins continues at a pace that is outrunning the ability of international institutions to craft adequate responses. With the total market capitalisation of dollar-pegged stablecoins having grown substantially over the past two years and showing no signs of slowing, the window for proactive intervention may be narrowing. Central bankers in the developing world are warning that the time to act is now, before the structural changes to their financial systems become irreversible.

The stakes, they argue, extend beyond monetary policy technicalities. At issue is the fundamental question of whether smaller economies retain meaningful financial autonomy in an era when a private digital token issued under US regulatory oversight can circulate freely within their borders, beyond the reach of their own laws and institutions.
